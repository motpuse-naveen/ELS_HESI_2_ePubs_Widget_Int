Movement, Coordination, and Sensory Input

Level of Consciousness

  • • Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) (Fig. 8.1)
    • — Used to assess level of consciousness (LOC)
    • — Maximum score 15, minimum 3
    • — Score of >8 = Good prognosis
    • — Score of <7 = Coma
    • — Score of 3 to 4 = High mortality rate
  • • A decrease in LOC may be the earliest sign of increasing intracranial pressure (ICP).
  • • Neurological Vital Signs (VSs)
    • — Pupil size
    • — Limb movement
    • — VSs
  • • Nursing Assessment
    • — Assess for early signs/symptoms of changes in LOC, which is the most sensitive and reliable indicator.
    • — Early signs
      • • ↓ GCS
      • • Change in orientation
    • — Late signs
      • • Cushing triad
        • • Widening pulse pressure
        • • Slowing heart rate with full bounding pulse
        • • Slowing and irregular respirations
      • • Change in size, response of pupils, dilated on side of injury initially
      • • Elevated temperature
      • • Posturing
      • • Assess for change in respiratory status
        • • Cheyne-Stokes respiration
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Maintain airway and provide adequate oxygenation.
    • — Severe ↓ LOC will need mechanical ventilation.
      • • Hyperventilate before suctioning.
      • • Limit suctioning to 15 seconds.
      • • Keep airway free of secretions.
      • • Prevent aspiration; turn head to side.
      • • Elevate head of bed (HOB) to 30 degrees to enhance respiration
    • — Treatment for increased ICP
      • • Prevent complications r/t sustained ↑ ICP
      • • ICP monitoring target: 5 to 15 mm Hg
      • • Elevate HOB 30 to 45 degrees to promote venous return
      • • Place the neck in a neutral position (not flexed or extended) to promote venous drainage
      • • Position client to avoid flexion of the hips, waist, and neck, especially to the right
      • • Implement measures to help client avoid Valsalva maneuver
    • — Controlled hyperventilation
    • — Mild hypothermia
    • — Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) drainage
    • — Infection is a serious complication, and systemic antibiotics are often ordered prophylactically
  • • Medications
    • — Hyperosmotic agents: 20% mannitol
    • — Steroids (↑ risk of infection)
      • • Dexamethasone
      • • Methylprednisolone
    • — Barbiturates
    • — Prophylactic phenytoin sodium
    • — Prophylactic levetiracetam
    • — Diuretics: Alternate with mannitol
    • — Avoid narcotics!

A chart for GCS shows appropriate stimulus and its respective response and score as follows: • Eyes open: Stimulus: Approach to bedside, verbal command, and pain. Response: Spontaneous response, opening of eyes to name or command, lack of opening of eyes to previous stimuli but opening to pain, lack of opening of eyes to any stimulus, and untestable. • Best verbal response: Stimulus: Verbal questioning with maximum arousal. Response: Appropriate orientation, conversant; correct identification of self, place, year, and month; confusion; conversant, but disorientation in one or more spheres; inappropriate or disorganized use of words (example: cursing), lack of sustained conversation; incomprehensible words, sounds (example: moaning); lack of sound, even with painful stimuli; and untestable. • Best motor response: Stimulus: Verbal command (example, raise your arm, hold up two fingers) and pain (pressure on proximal nail bed). Response: Obedience of command; localization of pain, lack of obedience but presence of attempts to remove offending stimulus; flexion withdrawal, flexion of arm in response to pain without abnormal flexion posture; abnormal flexion, flexing of arm at elbow and pronation, making a fist; abnormal extension, extension of arm at elbow usually with adduction and internal rotation of arm at shoulder; lack of response; and untestable.

Head Injury

  • • Symptoms
    • — Changes in LOC
    • — Signs of increased ICP
      • • Changes in VS
      • • Headache, amnesia
      • • Vomiting
      • • Pupillary changes (unequal/dilated)
      • • Seizure
      • • Ataxia
      • • Extremity motor strength and use: depressed or hyperactive
      • • Check for lacerations, bruises, skull depression, confusion, combativeness, personality changes
      • • Abnormal posturing (decorticate or decerebrate) (Fig. 8.2)
  • • CSF Leakage
    • Risk for meningitis increases with CSF leakage
    • Symptoms include: neck or shoulder pain, ringing in the ears (tinnitus), changes in hearing, dizziness, nausea or vomiting, changes in vision, changes in cognition or behavior
    • Drainage may come from nose (rhinorrhea) or ears (otorrhea)
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Neurological assessment every 15 minutes
    • — Notify health care practitioner (HCP) at first sign of deterioration
    • — Limit visitors
    • — Keep room quiet
    • — Prevent straining
    • — Keep HOB at 30 to 45 degrees
    • — Avoid neck flexion
    • — Monitor intake and output (I&O)

Cerebrovascular Accident

  • • Causes: a disruption in the blood supply to part of the brain. A medical emergency, strokes are classified as ischemic or hemorrhagic (Fig. 8.3).
    • — Transient ischemic attack (TIA): A transient episode of neurological dysfunction caused by the focal brain, spinal cord, or retinal ischemia but without acute infarction of the brain. Clinical symptoms typically last less than 1 hour. Most fully resolve.
    • — Ischemic stroke: a clot may be thromboembolic or embolic, causing minimal or absent blood flow to the neurons.
    • — Hemorrhagic stroke: may be a subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH; bleeding into the subarachnoid space) or an intracranial hemorrhage (direct bleeding into the brain tissue).
  • • Risk Factors
    • — Cardiovascular disease (CVD), hypertension, history of TIA
    • — Diabetes
    • — Advanced age, male, African American
    • — History of atrial fibrillation or flutter
    • — Oral contraceptives and hormone replacement therapy, antihistamine drugs
    • — Smoking
    • — Alcohol: >2 drinks/day; substance abuse, particularly cocaine
  • • Symptoms
    • — Motor loss: hemiparesis or hemiplegia
    • — Communication loss: dysarthria, dysphasia, aphasia, or apraxia
    • — Perceptual disturbance: visual, spatial, and sensory
    • — Impaired mental acuity or psychological loss: decreased attention span, memory loss, depression, lability, hostility
    • — Bladder dysfunction may be either incontinence or retention.
    • — Remember: Face, arms, speech, time (FAST)
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Time lost is brain lost
    • — Hemorrhagic or ischemic
    • — Computed tomography (CT) scan or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
    • — Prepare for thrombolytic therapy (limited therapeutic window; most effective within 3–4.5 hours)
    • — Monitor for signs and symptoms of increased ICP
    • — Monitor verbal ability and plan care appropriate to client’s ability to communicate
    • — Monitor swallowing to prevent aspiration
    • — Monitor for bowel and bladder control
    • — Monitor functional abilities
    • Mobility, activities of daily living (ADLs), elimination

Guillain-Barré Syndrome

  • • Causes
    • — Involves peripheral and cranial nerves in acute inflammatory demyelination process
    • — Usually occurs after an upper respiratory infection
  • • Symptoms
    • — Ascending paralysis that occurs rapidly
    • — Paralysis of respiratory system may occur quickly (Prepare to intubate)
    • — Tingling in fingers and toes
    • — Muscle weakness
    • — Severe lower back pain
    • — Facial paralysis
    • — Some clients experience a prolonged recovery time
  • • Diagnosis
    • — Spinal tap, possible nerve conduction tests, history, and assessment
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Plasmapheresis over 10 to 15 days
    • — IV high-dose immunoglobulin (Sandoglobulin) is as effective as plasma exchange and has the advantage of immediate availability and greater safety.
      • • Immunoglobulin client must be well hydrated and have adequate renal function.
    • — Maintain patent airway. Position with head to side. Keep intubation equipment at bedside.
    • — Reposition every 2 hours and PRN.
    • — Impaired swallowing may need:
    • Thickened liquids
    • Small, frequent feedings
    • TPN and NPO if gag reflex absent
    • — Provide psychological support.

Myasthenia Gravis

  • • A chronic neuromuscular autoimmune disease
  • • Fluctuating muscle weakness and abnormal fatigue of voluntary muscles, which increases throughout the day
  • • Symptoms
    • — Ocular muscle weakness
    • — Bulbar muscle weakness
    • — Skeletal muscle weakness
  • • Diagnosis
    • — Based on clinical presentation
      • • Muscle weakness
    • — Checking antibody levels
    • — Fine needle EMG
    • — CT of thymus
  • • Medications
    • — Anticholinesterase agents
      • • Try to achieve maximum strength and endurance
      • • Block action of cholinesterase
      • • Increase levels of ACH at junction
      • • Common medications: pyridostigmine (Mestinon), neostigmine (Prostigmin)
      • • Start with minimal doses
        • • Onset 30 minutes
        • • Duration 3 to 4 hours
        • • Must take on time!
    • — Corticosteroid: Prednisone
    • — Immunosuppressive agents: Azathioprine, cyclophosphamide
  • • Types of Crises
    • — Myasthenic Crisis
      • • Medical emergency!
      • • Neostigmine may be administered
      • • Caused by undermedication or infection
      • • Changes in VS, cyanosis, loss of cough and gag reflex, incontinence
      • • May require intubation
    • — Cholinergic Crisis
      • • Atropine may be administered.
      • • Results from overmedication
      • • Toxic levels of anticholinesterase medications
      • • Symptoms: abdominal cramps, diarrhea, excessive pulmonary secretions, n/v, blurred vision, pallor
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Coughing and deep-breathing exercises
    • — Suction equipment at bedside
    • — Sit upright when eating and for 1 hour afterward
    • — Keep chin downward when swallowing
    • — Plan activities and rest carefully; weakness worsens at the end of the day

Spinal Cord Injury

  • • Classification (Fig. 8.6)
    • — Extent of injury (complete, incomplete)
    • — Level of injury (skeletal, neurological)
    • — Mechanism of injury (i.e., flexion)
      • • Transection/partial transection
  • • Rule of Thumb
    • — Above C8 = quadriplegic
    • — Below C8 = paraplegic
  • • Symptoms
    • — Hypotension and bradycardia can occur in injuries above T6.
    • — Spinal shock: temporary loss of sensation, reflexes usually >48 hours, up to several weeks
  • • Flaccid paralysis/neurogenic shock
    • Complete loss of reflexes
    • Hypotension
    • Bradycardia
    • Bowel and bladder distention
    • Reverse as quickly as possible
  • • Autonomic dysreflexia
    • Medical emergency with injuries at or above T6
    • Notify charge nurse and HCP immediately
    • Exaggerated autonomic reflex response
    • Usually triggered by bowel or bladder distention
    • Signs and symptoms: severe headache, ↑BP, bradycardia, flushing, and profuse sweating
    • Elevate HOB (while maintaining correct alignment)
    • Relieve bowel or bladder distention
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Start with the ABCs: priority nursing assessment!
    • — Determine quality of respiratory rate
    • — Check neurological status
    • — Assess VSs
    • — Assess for severe headache and profuse sweating
    • — Assess bladder and bowel function; assess for paralytic ileus
    • — Assess psychosocial status
    • — Immobilize and stabilize
      • • Keep neck and body in anatomical alignment
      • • Maintain patent airway
      • • High-dose corticosteroids are used to control edema in first 24 hours
      • • Spinal shock: temporary loss of sensation and reflexes
  • • Post–spinal Shock
    • — Rehabilitation
    • — Monitor for paralytic ileus
      • • Assess bowel sounds
    • — Kinetic bed to promote blood flow, range-of-motion (ROM) exercises
    • — Antiembolism stockings, sequential compression devices
    • — Protect from skin breakdown
    • — Psychosocial assessments
    • — Bowel and bladder training
      • • Keep the bladder empty, and urine dilute and acidic to prevent urinary tract infection

Amputation

  • • Postoperative Care
    • — Monitor surgical dressing for drainage
    • — Proper body alignment
      • • Elevate the residual limb (stump) first 24 hours
      • • Do not elevate after 48 hours
    • — Provide passive ROM
    • — Encourage prone position periodically to decrease risk of contracture
    • — Proper stump bandaging to prepare for prosthesis
    • — Coordination of care with occupational therapist, physical therapist, and social worker
    • — Assess and address grieving
    • — Administer analgesics and antibiotics
    • —REMEMBER: Phantom pain is real pain. Treatment typically starts with pharmacological interventions, as well as psychological.

Osteoporosis

  • • Risk Factors
    • — Small-boned postmenopausal females, Caucasian, Asian
    • — Number of pregnancies, breastfeeding
    • — Family history
    • — Diet low in calcium
    • — Excessive alcohol, tobacco, and caffeine
    • — Sedentary lifestyle
    • — Low testosterone level in men
    • — Diabetes, cirrhosis, corticosteroids, loop diuretics
  • • Symptoms
    • — Dowager hump (outward curvature of the upper spine)
    • — Kyphosis of the dorsal spine
    • — Loss of height
    • — Pathological fractures
    • — Compression fracture of spine can occur
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Keep the bed in low position
    • — Provide adequate lighting
    • — Avoid using throw rugs
    • — Assist with ambulation
    • — Follow regular exercise program
    • — Diet high in vitamin D, protein, and calcium
    • — 20 minutes of UV exposure per day
    • — Routine DEXA screening begins at age 60
  • • Drug Therapy
    • — Bisphosphonates
      • • Alendronate, ibandronate, pamidronate, risedronate
    • — Selective estrogen receptor modulators
      • • Raloxifene HCL
      • • Teriparatide
    • — Recombinant parathyroid hormone
      • • Teriparatide

Rheumatoid Arthritis

  • • Chronic, systemic, progressive deterioration of the connective tissue (Fig. 8.8)
  • • Etiology: unknown, believed to be autoimmune
  • • Young to middle age; more females than males.
  • • Symptoms
    • — Systemic with exacerbations and remissions
    • — Small joints first, then spreads (Fig. 8.9)
    • — Joint pain, stiffness (may decrease with use) (Fig. 8.10)
    • — Decreased ROM
    • — Elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
    • — Positive rheumatoid factor (RF) in 80% of clients
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Drug Therapy
      • • High-dose ASA or nonsteroidal antiinflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
      • • Systemic corticosteroids
      • • Disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs)
        • • Methotrexate
        • • Sulfasalazine
        • • Hydroxychloroquine
        • • Leflunomide
        • • Adalimumab
    • — Management
      • • Heat application
      • • Weight management
      • • Rest and joint protection to maintain function
      • • Use assistive devices
        • • Shower chair
        • • Canes, walkers
        • • Straight-back chairs, elevated seats

Degenerative Joint Disease (Osteoarthritis) (Fig. 8.12)

  • • Symptoms
    • —Joint pain increases with activity
    • —Morning stiffness
    • —Crepitus
    • —Limited movement
    • —Joint enlargements (Fig. 8.13)
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Follow weight-reduction diet.
    • — Excessive use of involved joint may accelerate degeneration.
    • — Use proper body mechanics.
    • — Keep joints in functional position.
    • — Hot and cold applications for pain and stiffness
    • — NSAIDs, opioid analgesics, and intra-articular corticosteroids

Glaucoma

  • • Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)
    • —Most common form of primary glaucoma
    • —Develops slowly, with gradual loss of visual fields
    • —Outflow of aqueous humor through the chamber angle is reduced
    • —Aqueous humor flow is reduced in the trabecular meshwork; IOP gradually increases
    • —Silent thief of vision
  • • Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma (PACG)
    • — Sudden onset and is an emergency
    • — Forward displacement of the iris, which presses against the cornea and closes the chamber angle, suddenly preventing outflow of aqueous humor
    • — Sudden, excruciating pain in or around the eye
  • • Symptoms and Diagnosis
    • — Loss of peripheral vision
    • — May see halos around lights
    • — Diagnosed with tonometer to measure intraocular pressure
  • • Keys to Treatment
    • — ↓ Intraocular pressure
    • — ↓ Aqueous humor production
    • — ↑ Drainage of aqueous humor
    • — Reinforce teaching the client and family proper eye drop instillation and how to avoid activities that can increase intraocular pressure.
  • • Surgical Management
    • — Laser trabeculoplasty
    • — Trabeculoplasty
  • • Medications
    • — Prostaglandin agonists
      • • — β-Adrenergic blockers
      • • — α-Adrenergic agonists
      • • — Cholinergic agents (miotics)
      • • — Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors

Cataracts

  • • Clouding or opacity of the lens due to trauma, aging, or congenital defect (Fig. 8.14)
  • • Early Signs
    • — Blurred vision
    • — Decreased color perception
  • • Late Signs
    • — Double vision
    • — Clouded pupil
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Preoperative
      • • Assess current medications being taken.
      • • Stop anticoagulants before surgery.
      • • Reinforce teaching on the technique to instill eye drops.
    • — Postoperative
      • • Wear eye shield when sleeping.
      • • Avoid lifting >10 lb.
      • • Avoid lying on operative side.
      • • Report signs of ↑ IOP
        • • Loss of peripheral vision
        • • Visual field defects
      • • Acute pain is an abnormal finding.

Eye Trauma/Injury
  • • Detached Retina
    • — Described as curtain falling over visual field
    • — Painless with sudden loss of vision
    • — May see black spots or floaters (indicates bleeding has occurred with detachment)
    • — Retina repaired surgically
    • — Keep eye patch over affected area
  • • Trauma
    • — Determine the type of injury.
    • — Early notification of HCP
    • — Position client in sitting position to reduce IOP.
    • — Never attempt to remove embedded object.
    • — Irrigate eye if a chemical injury has occurred.

Hearing Loss
  • • Conductive Hearing Loss
    • — Sounds do not travel to the inner ear
    • — May benefit from hearing aid
    • — Cerumen buildup
  • • Sensorineural Hearing Loss
    • — Sound distorted by defect in inner ear
    • — Common causes:
      • • Infections
      • • Ototoxic drugs
        • • Gentamicin
        • • Vancomycin
        • • Furosemide
      • • Trauma
      • • Aging process
  • • Symptoms
    • — Inability to hear a whisper from 1 to 2 feet
    • — Shouting in conversations
    • — Turning head to favor one ear
    • — Loud volume on television
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — When speaking with the client, face him or her directly.
    • — Ensure the client is wearing hearing aids and eyeglasses, if appropriate.
    • — Speak at a normal tone; enunciate words.
    • — Rephrase questions instead of repeating them.
    • — Do not cover mouth with hand when speaking.

Test-Taking Strategy

Take the NCLEX as soon as possible after graduation to ensure that everything you’ve learned in your nursing program is still in your long-term and short-term memory. Small details of complex concepts will become vague in your memory over time. Taking your NCLEX is a personal decision; however, we strongly recommend that you prepare well for your NCLEX and take the exam soon as possible after graduation, knowing that a given Authorization to Test (ATT) is valid for 90 days. Therefore, you must take the exam within that time frame once your ATT is obtained.
