7

Regulatory, Reproductive, and Urinary

Obesity

  • • Occurs when calorie intake exceeds the body’s metabolic rate.
  • • Assessed Using Body Mass Index (BMI) (Fig. 7.1)
    • — Normal weight: BMI of 18.5 to 24.9 kg/m2
    • — Overweight: BMI of 25 to 29.9 kg/m2
    • — Obese: BMI ≥30 kg/m2
    • — Morbidly obese: BMI >40 kg/m2
  • • Abdominal and visceral fat have been linked to metabolic syndrome.
  • • Disproportionally represented in minority populations.
  • • Nursing Assessment
    • — Risk factor screening
    • — Cardiovascular disease (CVD)
    • — Hypertension
    • — Sleep apnea
    • — Type 2 diabetes
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Lifestyle Management
      • • Medical/nutritional therapy
      • • Physical activity
      • • Behavior modification
    • — Pharmacological Therapy
      • • Orlistat blocks fat breakdown in GI tract
    • — Bariatric Surgery
      • • Criteria for bariatric surgery:
      • • BMI ≥40 or ≥35 kg/m2 with 1+ severe, obesity-related complications
      • • Gastric bypass, gastric banding, Roux-en-Y
  • • Metabolic Syndrome
    • — A collection of risk factors that ↑ an individual’s chance of developing CVD and diabetes mellitus
    • — Meets three or more criteria:
      • • Waist circumference ≥40 inches (102 cm) in men or ≥35 inches (89 cm) in women
      • • Triglycerides (TG) >160 mg/dL (>1.81mmol/L) or drug treatment for elevated triglycerides
      • • High-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol <45 mg/dL (<0.75 mmol/L) in men or <55 mg/dL (<0.91 mmol/L) in women or drug treatment for ↓HDL
      • • Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) >130 mg/dL (>3.37 mmol/L)
      • • Blood pressure (BP) ≥130 mm Hg systolic or ≥85 mm Hg diastolic or drug treatment for hypertension
      • • Fasting blood glucose (FBG) level ≥100 mg/dL (>10 mmol/L) or drug treatment for elevated glucose

Prediabetes

  • • ↑ Risk for developing diabetes.
    • — Individuals are at increased risk of developing diabetes. Blood glucose is elevated but not enough to meet the diagnostic criteria for diabetes
  • • Assessment
    • — FBG level: 100 to 125 mg/dL (5.55–6.94 mmol/L)
    • — 2-hour oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) values: 130 to 199 mg/dL (7.21–11.04 mmol/L)
    • —  Glycosylated hemoglobin (HgbA1c): 5.7% to 6.4%

Diabetes Mellitus

  • • Chronic, multisystem disease related to abnormal insulin production, impaired insulin utilization, or both
  • • Diagnostic Criteria
    • — HgbA1c >6.5%
    • — FBG level >126 mg/dL (>6.9 mmol/L)
    • —   2-hour OGTT (75 g glucose load): >200 mg/dL (>11.10 mmol/L)
    • —  Random blood glucose level >200 mg/dL (>11.10 mmol/L) accompanied by classic symptoms of hyperglycemia
  • • Type 1
    • — Immune-related disease— Absolute insulin deficiency — T cells destroy ß cells
    • — Rapid onset
    • — Occurs at any age, typically in childhood or adolescence
    • — Classic symptoms: abrupt onset of polyuria, polydipsia, and weight loss
      • • Nausea, vomiting, dehydration, abdominal pain, Kussmaul respirations, acetone odor to breath
      • • Ketoacidosis possible
      • • Weakness and fatigue
    • — Laboratory Tests:
      • • Serum glucose ≥250 mg/dL (>14.0 mmol/L)
      • • Ketonuria in large amounts
      • • Arterial pH of <7.30 and HCO3 <15 mEq/L (<15 mmol/L)
  • • Type 2
    • — Insulin resistance
    • — Relative insulin deficiency
    • — Insulin production
    • — Excessive or unregulated glucose production from liver
    • — Risk Factors
      • • Age ≥45 years
      • • Overweight (BMI >25)
      • • First-degree relative with diabetes
      • • Sedentary lifestyle
      • • Ethnic at-risk group
      • • Gestational diabetes or a baby >9 lb (>4.1 kg)
      • • CVD or hypertension
      • • Abnormal lipids (low HDL, high TG)
      • • Prediabetes
      • • Polycystic ovary syndrome
      • • Signs of insulin resistance (acanthosis nigricans)
    • — Insidious onset with the following:
      • • 3 Ps (polyuria, polyphagia, polydipsia) + fatigue + weight loss
      • • Recurrent infections
      • • Prolonged wound healing
      • • Visual changes
      • • Impotence
      • • Rare: ketoacidosis; more likely hyperglycemic hyperosmolar nonketotic syndrome with blood sugar (BS) >600 mg/dL (>33.3 mmol/L)
      • • Dehydration, plasma hyperosmolality, altered mental state, and absent ketone bodies
  • • Other Types
    • — Cystic fibrosis related
    • — Transplant related
    • — Gestational
    • — Steroid induced
    • — Hospital-related hyperglycemia
  • • Complications
    • — Stroke, hypertension
    • — Dermopathy
    • — Atherosclerosis
    • — Nephropathy
    • — Retinopathy, cataracts, glaucoma, blindness
    • — Coronary artery disease
    • — Gastroparesis
    • — Islet cell loss
    • — Peripheral vascular atherosclerosis
    • — Gangrene
    • — Erectile dysfunction
    • — Peripheral neuropathy
    • — Neurogenic bladder
  • • Nursing Assessment
    • —  Integument: skin breakdown, poor wound healing, ulcerations on extremities and thick nails
    • — Eyes: retinal problems, cataracts
    • — Kidneys: edema, urinary retention
    • — Periphery: cool skin
    • —   Cardiopulmonary angina and dyspnea
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • —   Medical Nutrition Therapy
      • • Type 1: integrate insulin with eating and exercise
      • • Type 2: heart-healthy diet and moderate weight loss of 10% to 20%; medications may be needed if blood glucose cannot be controlled with diet and exercise
      • • —   Antidiabetics, statins, insulin
    • — Education
      • • Reinforce teaching on injection techniques, finger stick BS (FSBS) testing techniques, calibration, and record keeping.
      • • Refrigerate unopened insulin.
      • • Eat heart-healthy diet with consistent carbohydrate intake, proteins, and saturated fats.
      • • Integrate meals and snacks, insulin therapy, and physical activity (snack before exercise).
      • • Monitor for signs and symptoms of hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia.
      • • Foot care: check daily/report injuries.
      • • Manage sick days.
      • • —   Continue insulin.
      • • —  Check BS more frequently.
      • • —  Watch for signs and symptoms of hyperglycemia.

Thyroid Gland Feedback Loop

  • • Hypothalamus releases thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH)
    • —  TRH triggers the anterior pituitary
    • —  Pituitary releases thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH)
    • —  Thyroid gland releases thyroid hormones T3 and T4

Hyperthyroidism

  • • Nursing Assessment
    • — Enlarged thyroid gland (Fig. 7.3)
    • — Exophthalmos (Fig. 7.4)
    • — Weight loss
    • — T3 elevated
    • — T4 elevated
    • — Diarrhea
    • — Tachycardia
    • — Bruit over thyroid
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Diet: high protein, high calorie, low caffeine, low fiber
    • — Treatment may trigger hypothyroidism; client may need hormone replacement
    • —  Propylthiouracil therapy to block the synthesis of T3 and T4
    • — Iodine (131I) therapy to destroy thyroid cells
  • • Surgical Management
    • — Thyroidectomy
      • • Position in high Fowler.
      • • Assess behind neck for drainage.
      • • Support neck when moving client.
      • • Monitor for laryngeal edema.
      • • Place tracheotomy set, oxygen, and suction equipment at bedside.
      • • Place calcium gluconate at bedside (because of possible damage or removal of parathyroid gland).

Hypothyroidism

  • • Nursing Assessment
    • — Fatigue
    • — Bradycardia
    • — Weight gain
    • — Constipation
    • — Periorbital edema
    • — Cold intolerance
    • — High TSH (over 10 mU/L)
    • — Low T3 (<70 ng/dL)
    • — Low T4 (<4 ng/dL)
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • —  Be alert for myxedema coma, an acute exacerbation of hypothyroidism.
    • —  Maintain airway.
    • — Reinforce medication regimen.
    • — Monitor for side effects of medications.
    • — Monitor for signs and symptoms of constipation.
  • • Thyroid Medications
    • — Levothyroxine (Synthroid)
      • • Take before food in a.m. (on an empty stomach).
      • • Monitor heart rate.
      • • Hold for pulse >100 beats/min.
    • — Liothyronine (Cytomel)
      • • Increases metabolic rate.
      • • Acts as synthetic T3.
      • • Check hormone levels regularly.
      • • Avoid food containing iodine.
    • — Levothyroxine (T4) + liothyronine (T3)
      • • Rapid onset

Addison Disease

  • • Causes
    • — Sudden withdrawal from corticosteroids
    • — Hypofunction of adrenal cortex
    • —  Lack of pituitary adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH)
  • • Symptoms
    • — Weight loss, N/V, anorexia
    • — Hypovolemia, hypoglycemia
    • — Hyponatremia, hyperkalemia
    • — Loss of body hair
    • — Postural hypotension
    • — Hyperpigmentation
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Check vital signs frequently
    • — Daily weights
    • — Monitor serum electrolytes and glucose
    • — Monitor for muscle weakness
    • — Diet: ↑ sodium, ↓ potassium, ↑ carbohydrates
    • — Intake: at least 3 L of fluid per day
    • — Monitor for symptoms of overdosage or underdosage of corticosteroid and mineralocorticoid therapy
    • — Wear medic-alert bracelet.
    • — Instruct client to carry emergency kit with 100 mg IM corticosteroid.

Cushing Syndrome

  • • Causes
    • —  Excess adrenal corticoid activity caused by adrenal, pituitary, or hypothalamus tumors. Most common cause is administration of exogenous corticosteroids.
  • • Symptoms (Fig. 7.5)
    • —  Moon face and edema of lower extremities
    • —  Flat affect, irritability, anxiety, depression, psychosis
    • —  Truncal obesity with abdominal striae
    • —  Buffalo hump (fat deposits)
    • —  Muscle atrophy, weakness
    • —  Thin, dry, pale skin
    • —  Hypertension
    • —  Osteoporosis
    • —  Immunosuppressed
    • —  Hypervolemia
    • —  Hirsutism
  • • Laboratory Results
    • — Hyperglycemia
    • — Hypocalcemia
    • — Hypernatremia
    • — Hypokalemia
    • — Increased plasma cortisol levels
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Monitor for signs and symptoms of infection
      • • Fever
      • • Skin lesions
      • • Elevated white blood cell counts (WBCs)
    • — Avoid extreme temperatures, infections, and emotional disturbances
    • — Wear medic-alert bracelet
    • — Diet: low sodium; low carbohydrate

Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs)

  • • Symptoms and treatment vary by disease.
  • • Reinforce Teaching Safer Sex:
    • — Limit number of partners
    • — Abstinence, mutual monogamy
    • — Use latex condoms.
    • — Lifestyle risks: drugs and alcohol
  • • Report incidence of STIs to appropriate health agencies.
  • • Syphilis
    • — Primary (local, chancre is a highly contagious red painless lesion)
    • — Secondary (occurs 6 weeks–6 months after exposure; generalized rash that affects palms of hands and soles of feet)
    • — Tertiary (occurs 10–30 years postexposure resulting in cardiac and neurologic destruction)
    • — Diagnosis with venereal disease research laboratory or fluorescent treponemal antibody absorption
    • — Treatment: Penicillin G IM (2.4 million units); if allergic to penicillin, use tetracycline
  • • Gonorrhea
    • — Diagnosis via cultures or smears
    • — Females are usually asymptomatic; males experience dysuria, yellowish-green urethral discharge, and urinary frequency
    • — Treat with azithromycin
  • • Chlamydia
    • — Usually asymptomatic in females but may have dysuria, vaginal discharge elevated temperature, and uterine or adnexal tenderness.
    • — Diagnosis via culture
  • • Trichomonas
    • — Caused by infection with Trichomonas vaginalis (a protozoan parasite).
    • — Usually no symptoms are experienced; when experienced, symptoms include:
      • • Itching, burning, redness or soreness of the genitals
      • • Dysuria
      • • A clear, white, yellowish, or greenish discharge
    • — Diagnosis: parasite can be seen under a microscope
  • • Candidiasis
    • —  Genital yeast infection can be transmitted sexually between partners.
  • • Herpes: Herpes Simplex Virus 2
    • — Vesicles in clusters rupture and leave painful erosions that make urination painful
    • — Treat with Acyclovir—it partially controls symptoms. Use viscous lidocaine topically to provide palliative care to relieve pain
  • • Human Papillomavirus (HPV)
    • — There are 70 strains implicated in cervical cancer—increasing in adolescent population
    • — Lesions are either small, wartlike, clustered, flat, or raised; routine vaccine before onset of sexual activity.
    • — Treat with topical podophyllum (contraindicated in pregnancy); laser, cryotherapy
  • • Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)/AIDS
    • —  See Chapter 3 for detailed information.

Benign Uterine Tumors (Fibroids)

  • • Arise from uterine muscle tissue
    • — Fertility issues
    • — Menorrhagia
    • — Uterine enlargement
    • — Dysmenorrhea
    • — Anemia
    • — Low back and pelvic pain
    • — Tend to disappear after menopause
  • • Nonsurgical option: magnetic resonance–guided focused ultrasound
  • • Surgical options: Myomectomy, hysterectomy

Erectile Dysfunction (ED)
  • • The inability to attain or maintain an erect penis that allows satisfactory sexual performance; ED can result from a number of factors.Etiology
    • — Diabetes
    • — Vascular disease
    • — Side effects of medications
    • — Result of surgery (prostatectomy)
    • — Trauma
    • — Chronic illness
    • — Decreased gonadal hormone secretion
    • — Stress
    • — Depression
    • — Drugs and alcohol
  • • Oral Drug Therapy
    • — The treatment for ED is based on the underlying cause.
    • — Sildenafil citrate
      • • Dosage: 25 to 100 mg PO, 1 dose/day, 1 hour before sexual activity
      • • May potentiate the hypotensive effect of nitrates; contraindicated for individuals taking nitrates (e.g., nitroglycerin).
      • • Reinforce to client to seek medical attention for an erection lasting longer than 4 hours (priapism).
      • • Use with caution in elderly because drugs are slow to metabolize and be excreted.

Test-Taking Strategy

Read the entire scenario and question before focusing on the answer options. Candidates who do not thoroughly read the questions may miss keywords/phrases within the scenario or misinterpret the question’s focus. When you are preparing for or actually taking the NCLEX exam, make sure you understand what the question is really asking.
