Clinical Concepts and Mechanisms of Disease

Preoperative Care

  • • Preparation
    • — Obtain a complete history, including the following:
      • • Physical assessment
      • • List of current medications (prescriptions, over the counter [OTC], complementary, and supplements)
      • • List all allergies and reactions
      • • Previous surgical experiences (response to anesthetic)
      • • Signed consent (confirm informed consent before client is sedated)
      • • Confirm labs and diagnostics
      • • Skin preparation
      • • Preop checklist and pre-procedure verification
  • • Reinforce teaching:
    • — Diet restrictions per prescription
    • — Discuss medications that should/or should not be taken the day of surgery
    • — Controlled coughing and deep breathing
    • — Incentive spirometry use
    • — Activities (leg exercises, turning, mobility, performing active and passive range-of-motion [ROM] exercises and repositioning)
    • — Use of thromboembolic deterrent (TED) stockings, sequential compression devices (SCDs), and expected outcomes
    • — Review methods of pain control
    • — Complete the preop verification checklist

Postoperative Care (Fig. 3.1)

  • • ABCs and hemodynamic stability (monitor for signs and symptoms of shock)
  • • Position client supine or in low Fowler, if not contraindicated.
  • • Manage pain.
  • • Maintain core body temperature.

  •  
    • Prevent Common Complications—  Urinary retention

    • — Urinary tract infection (UTI)
      • • Remove urinary catheter postoperative day (POD) #1 or #2, unless contraindicated
    • — Pulmonary problems
      • • Check breath sounds, O2 saturation
    • — Gastrointestinal (GI)
      • • Absent bowel sounds
      • • Decreased peristalsis
      • • Paralytic ileus
    • —  Wound management
      • • Incisions, drains, skin integrity
      • • Wound dehiscence or evisceration
    • — Venous thromboembolism (VTE) prophylaxis
      • • Thromboembolic deterrent (TED) hose or sequential compression devices (SCDs)
      • • Pharmacological agents for VTE prophylaxis
      • • Early mobilization (Fig 3.2)

Pain

  • • Pain management is client centered.
  • • Pain is whatever the client says it is, existing whenever he or she says it does.
  • • Pain occurs in all clinical settings.
  • • Nurses have a legal and ethical responsibility for managing clients’ pain and suffering (be aware of any personal bias or misconceptions about pain management).
  • • Assessment includes:
    • — P: Precipitating or palliative
    • — Q: Quality
    • — R: Relief measures/region (location)
    • —   S: Severity (using a scale appropriate for age and client condition: numerical, descriptive, FACES, Oucher)
    • — T: Timing (onset, duration)
    • — U: Effect of pain on client
  • • Observe for nonverbal behaviors that can indicate pain.
    • — Moaning
    • — Grimacing
    • — Clenching teeth
    • — Pacing
    • — Inactivity
  • • Wong-Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale recommended for persons aged 3 years and older (Fig. 3.3B).
  • • Assess personal, cultural, spiritual, and ethical beliefs that may influence the perception of pain.
  • • Special populations: palliative care, end-of-life
  • • Documentation includes pain assessment data (preintervention and postintervention rating), interventions (both nonpharmacological and pharmacological), and reinforcing client teaching.
  • • Controlled substance disposal must be documented per regulatory guidelines (ask another nurse to witness the waste of a pill fragment, discarded injection contents, or discarded liquid contents).

Fluids and Electrolytes

  • • Fluid Balance
    • — Changes in osmolarity cause shifts in fluid. The osmolarity of the extracellular fluid (ECF) is almost entirely due to sodium.
    • — The osmolarity of intracellular fluid (ICF) is related to many particles, with potassium being the primary electrolyte.
    • — The pressures in the ECF and the ICF are almost identical. If either ECF or ICF changes in concentration, fluid shifts from the area of lesser concentration to the area of greater concentration.
  • • Fluid Volume Excess
    • — Causes
      • • Heart failure (most common), renal failure, cirrhosis, overhydration
    • — Symptoms
      • • Peripheral edema (Fig. 3.4), periorbital edema, elevated blood pressure (BP), dyspnea, altered level of consciousness (LOC)
    • — Laboratory findings
      • • ↓ Blood urea nitrogen (BUN), ↓ Hemoglobin (Hgb), ↓ Hematocrit (Hct), ↓ serum osmolality, ↓ urine specific gravity
    • — Treatment
      • • Diuretics, fluid restrictions, weigh daily, monitor K+

A closeup of the left lower leg shows edema in with a pit created by a finger present at the lateral side of the lower leg

  • • Fluid Volume Deficit
    • — Causes
      • • Inadequate fluid intake, hemorrhage, vomiting, diarrhea, burns, massive edema
    • — Symptoms
      • • Weight loss, postural hypotension, tachycardia, dry mucous membranes
    • — Laboratory findings
      • • ↑ BUN and ↑ or normal creatinine, ↑ Hgb, ↑ Hct, ↑ urine specific gravity
    • — Treatment
      • • Strict intake and output (I&O), replace with isotonic fluids, monitor BP, weigh daily
  • • Types of IV Fluids
    • — Isotonic fluids expand the ECF volume (osmolarity between 250 and 300 mOsm/L): 0.9% normal saline (NS), lactated Ringer (LR) solution.
    • — Hypotonic fluids move water into the cell by osmosis, causing them to swell (osmolarity less than 250 mOsm/L): 0.45% NS.
    • — Hypertonic fluids pull fluid from cells, causing them to shrink (osmolarity >300 mOsm/L): D10; 3% saline, D5 in 0.45% NS.

Electrolyte Imbalances

Sodium: Skeletal muscle contraction, cardiac contraction, nerve impulse transmission, normal osmolarity, and volume of ECF

  • • Hyponatremia
    • — Causes: diuretics, GI fluid loss, hypotonic IV fluids, diaphoresis
    • — Diagnostic: Na+ < 136 mEq/L (<136 mmol/L)
    • — Signs and symptoms: muscle cramps, confusion, weakness, seizures
  • • —   Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • • Check BP frequently
    • • Restrict fluids
    • • Diet (high sodium content)
      • • Cheese
      • • Table salt
      • • Seafood
      • • Processed meat
      • • Canned foods
      • • Ketchup
      • • Salty snack foods
  • • Hypernatremia
    • — Causes: water deprivation, diabetes insipidus, renal failure, Cushing syndrome
    • —   Signs and symptoms: dry mucous membranes, thick mucus, low urinary output, decreased skin turgor, restlessness, agitation, confusion, flushed skin, pulmonary edema, seizures, thirst, fever
    • — Diagnostic: Na+ > 145 mEq/L (>145 mmol/L)
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • No IVs that contain sodium
      • • Restrict dietary sodium
      • • Weigh daily

Potassium: Depolarizes and generates action potentials, regulating protein synthesis and glucose use and storage

  • • Hypokalemia
    • — Causes: diuretics, vomiting, diarrhea, Cushing syndrome, gastric suction
    • — Diagnostic: K+ < 3.5 mEq/L (mmol/L)
    • — Signs and symptoms: rapid, thready pulse, flat T-waves, anorexia, muscle cramps, weakness, fatigue, tingling, numbness, nausea, vomiting, constipation, GI bloating, palpitations
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • Administer potassium by mouth (PO) as ordered
      • • Diet: foods high in K+
        • • Oranges
        • • Bananas
        • • Apricots
        • • Cantaloupe
        • • Legumes
        • • Leafy vegetables
        • • Potatoes
        • • Meat
        • • Salt substitute
  • • Hyperkalemia
    • — Causes: oliguria, acidosis, renal failure, Addison disease
    • — Diagnostic: K+ >5 mEq/L (>5 mmol/L)
    • — Signs and symptoms: tall tented T-waves, bradycardia, muscle weakness, tiredness, numbness, tingling, nausea, vomiting, respiratory distress, angina, palpitations, irregular heartbeat
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • 10 U of regular insulin and 50 mL of dextrose 50% in water (D50W)
      • • Sodium polystyrene sulfonate is used to treat hyperkalemia
      • • IV loop diuretics
      • • Renal dialysis

Calcium: Maintains bone strength and density, activates enzymes, allows skeletal and cardiac muscle contraction, controls nerve impulse transmission, and allows for blood clottingHypocalcemia

    • — Causes: renal failure, hypoparathyroidism malabsorption, pancreatitis, alkalosis
    • — Diagnostic: Ca++ <9 mg/dL (<2.25 mmol/L)
    • — Signs and symptoms: positive Trousseau sign (Fig. 3.5) (carpopedal spasm that occurs when a BP cuff is inflated above SBP for 3 minutes); diarrhea; numbness; convulsions; numbness and tingling of fingers, toes, and circumoral (around mouth) region; positive Chvostek sign (Fig. 3.6) (contraction of facial muscles when facial nerve is tapped); hyperactive reflexes; muscle twitching and cramping; carpal and pedal spasms; tetany; seizures; laryngospasm; dysrhythmias

  • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • • Administer supplements PO as ordered.
    • • Encourage calcium-rich foods, vitamin D, protein.
    • • For acute hypocalcemia, keep a tracheostomy tray and resuscitation bag at bedside in case of laryngeal spasms.
  • • Hypercalcemia
    • — Causes: hyperparathyroidism, malignant bone disease, excessive supplementation
    • — Diagnostic: Ca++ >10.5 mg/dL (>2.62 mmol/L)
    • — Signs and symptoms: muscle weakness, constipation, nausea and vomiting, dysrhythmias, behavioral changes, anorexia, fatigue, diminished reflexes, lethargy, decreased level of consciousness, confusion, personality change, cardiac arrest if severe
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • Limit vitamin D intake
      • • Avoid calcium-based antacids
      • • Administer calcitonin to reduce calcium
      • • Renal dialysis may be required

Magnesium: Controls skeletal muscle contraction, carbohydrate metabolism, adenosine triphosphate [ATP] formation, vitamin activation, and cell growth

  • • Hypomagnesemia
    • — Causes: alcoholism, malabsorption, diabetic ketoacidosis, diuretics
    • — Diagnostic: Mg2+ <1.3 mEq/L (<0.65 mmol/L)
    • — Signs and symptoms: skeletal muscle weakness; hyperactive deep tendon reflexes; numbness and tingling; painful muscle contractions; decreased GI motility; nausea
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • Administer oral supplements
      • • Increase dietary intake of magnesium
      • • IV magnesium via infusion pump
      • • Monitor vital signs
  • • Hypermagnesemia
    • — Renal failure, adrenal insufficiency, excess replace-ment
    • — Mg2+ > 2.1 mEq/L (>1.05 mmol/L)
    • — Bradycardia; peripheral vasodilation; hypotension; prolonged PR interval with a widened QRS complex; decreased to absent deep tendon reflexes
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • No magnesium drugs for clients with chronic kidney disease
      • • IV administration of calcium chloride or calcium gluconate

Phosphorous: Activates vitamins and enzymes, forms ATP for energy supplies, assists in cell growth and metabolism, maintains acid-base balance and calcium homeostasis

  • • Hypophosphatemia
    • — Causes: alcohol withdrawal, diabetic ketoacidosis, respiratory alkalosis
    • — Diagnostic: Phosphate <3.0 mg/dL (<0.97 mmol/L)
    • — Signs and symptoms: ↓ Cardiac output, weak peripheral pulses, skeletal muscle weakness
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • • Oral supplementation
    • • Diet high in phosphorus
  • • Hyperphosphatemia
    • — Causes: renal failure, excess intake
    • — Diagnostic: Phosphate >4.5 mg/dL (>1.45 mmol/L)
    • — Signs and symptoms: include signs of hypocalcemia
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • Administer the phosphate binders as ordered
      • • Diet low in phosphorus

Acid-Base

Acid-Base Balance

  • • pH
    • — Normal 7.35–7.45
    • — <7.35 = acidosis
    • — >7.45 = alkalosis
  • • pCO2
    • — Normal 35–45 mmHg
    • — >45 = acidosis
    • — <35 = alkalosis
  • • HCO3
    • — Normal 21–28 mEq/L (21–28 mmol/L)
    • — <21 = acidosis
    • — >28 = alkalosis

Arterial Blood Gas Interpretation Practice

  1. 1. Determine whether the pH value is normal, acidic, or alkalotic.
    1. 1. 7.31 ______
    2. 2. 7.47 ______
    3. 3. 7.36 ______
  • Answer
    1. 1. 7.31 Acidic
    2. 2. 7.47 Alkalotic
    3. 3. 7.36 Normal
  1. 2. Determine whether the client is hypoventilating, hyperventilating, or has normal ventilation.
    1. 1. pCO2 = 42
    2. 2. pCO2 = 33
    3. 3. pCO2 = 55
  • Answer
    1. 1. pCO2 = 42, Normal ventilation
    2. 2. pCO2 = 33, Hyperventilating: blowing off CO2
    3. 3. pCO2 = 55, Hypoventilating: retaining CO2
  1. 3. Determine whether this reading indicates a state of acidosis, alkalosis, or normal.
    1. 1. HCO3 = 20
    2. 2. HCO3 = 33
    3. 3. HCO3 = 21
  • Answer
    1. 1. HCO3 = 20 Acidosis
    2. 2. HCO3 = 33 Alkalosis
    3. 3. HCO3 = 21 Normal

Steps for Interpreting ABG Results

  1. 1. Is there alkalosis or acidosis present? First look at pH results.
    • pH < 7.35 = acidosis
    • pH > 7.45 = alkalosis
  2. 2. What is the respiratory (pCO2) component?
    • • The number is the amount of CO2 in the blood
    • • CO2 is the respiratory component in the blood measured by millimeters of mercury (mm Hg)
    • • Normal CO2 level is between 35–45 mm Hg
    • • Respiratory alkalosis is pCO2 below 35 mm Hg; symptom is hyperventilation
    • • Respiratory acidosis is pCO2 above 45 mm Hg; related to hypoventilation, symptom is confusion or restlessness
  3. 3. Check your blood gas report for the bicarbonate (HCO3) level. It is the renal component in the acid-base balance. The normal level for these ions is from 22 to 26 mEq/L.
  4. 4. Determine whether there is compensation for the pH imbalance. Generally, the pulmonary and renal systems compensate for each other to return the pH to normal. The lungs will compensate for metabolic instability by changing CO2 excretion and the kidneys will compensate for the respiratory instability by altering bicarbonate retention and H+ secretion.

Safety: Sentinel Event

  • • An unexpected outcome involving a death or serious injury.
  • • Accredited hospitals are expected to identify and respond to all sentinel events.
  • • Examples of sentinel events include client suicide, wrong-site surgery, medication error, client fall, client death or injury in restraints, and transfusion error.

High-Alert Medications

  • • Most likely to cause significant harm to a client even when used as intended.
  • • Types of drugs
    • — Anticoagulants, narcotics and opiates, insulin, chemotherapeutic drugs, and sedatives
  • • Types of harm
    • — Hypotension, bleeding, hypoglycemia, delirium, lethargy, bradycardia, respiratory depression, or distress
  • • Strategies to prevent harm
    • — Built-in redundancies
    • — Double checking
    • — Smart pumps
    • — Standardized or protocol-driven order sets

Death and Grief

  • • Stages of Grief
    • — Denial
    • — Anger
    • — Bargaining
    • — Depression
    • — Acceptance
  • • Facilitate grieving process by using therapeutic touch, displaying warm and caring behaviors, and using open-ended statements to listen to and assist those grieving to understand their feelings and behaviors.
  • • Encourage the client to express anger.
  • • Do not remove the defense mechanisms for coping that the client uses in crisis.
  • • Customs surrounding death and dying vary among cultures.
  • • Make every attempt to understand and accommodate the family’s cultural traditions when caring for the dying client.

Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)

HIV Routes of Transmission

  • • —  Unprotected sexual contact
  • • —  Exposure to blood through drug-using equipment
  • • —  Perinatal transmission
  • • —  Can occur during pregnancy, at time of delivery, or after birth through breastfeeding

HIV Diagnosis

  • • — Laboratory Testing for HIV
    • Antibody tests
      • • Look for antibodies to HIV in a person’s blood or oral fluid (e.g., enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay [ELISA]).
    • Antigen/antibody tests
      • • Look for both HIV antibodies and antigens.
    • NATs (nucleic acid tests)
      • • Look for the actual virus in the blood.
    • In-home HIV test: Positive result is only preliminary; it must be confirmed by an HCP
  • • Diagnosis of AIDS
    • — HIV positive and a CD4+ T-cell count < 200 cells/mm3 or an opportunistic infection

HIV Drug Therapy

  • • — HIV Drug Therapy Goals
    • Reduce the viral load
    • Maintain or raise CD4+ T-cell count
    • To delay the development of the HIV-related symptoms and opportunistic diseases
  • • — Side Effects
    • Multiple drug interactions are possible between nucleoside reverse transcriptase (NTRIs) and other drugs
  • • — HIV Medications
    • Nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs)
    • Nonnucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NNRTIs)
    • Protease inhibitors
    • Integrase inhibitors
    • Fusion inhibitors
    • Entry inhibitor
    • Postattachment inhibitors

  • • — No smoking/alcohol/drugs

Pediatric HIV

  • • — Pediatric HIV: Clinical Manifestations
    • Recurrent infections such as thrush (oral candidiasis)
    • Unexplained fever
    • Lymphadenopathy
    • Hepatosplenomegaly
    • Failure to thrive
    • Developmental delay
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Family education focuses on transmission and control of infectious diseases.
    • — Safety issues include appropriate storage of special medications and equipment management.
    • — Aggressive pain management
    • — Common psychological concerns include disclosure of the diagnosis.
    • — Prevention
    • — The focus of nursing interventions is preventing infection, promoting proper nutrition, promoting self-care, and supporting counseling efforts.

  • • Breast: Breast self-examination (BSE) (early 20s; educate about the benefits and limitations); emphasis is on reporting any new breast symptoms to the HCP; clinical breast exam (CBE) every 3 years and annually after age 40; mammography biannually at age 40 until age 74

    • — Women ages 21–29: Pap test alone every 3 years
  • • — HPV testing alone can be considered for women ages 25–29, but Pap tests are preferred— Women ages 30–65: have three options for testingOption 1: Pap test and an HPV test (co-testing) every 5 years. Option 2: Pap test alone every 3 years. Option 3: HPV testing alone every 5 years.

  • • Prostate: Digital rectal examination (DRE) and prostate-specific antigen test (PSA) for men age 50 at average risk of developing prostate cancer

Test-Taking Strategy

When analyzing a question: 1) eliminate all the options you can; 2) choose the best answer to the extent of your knowledge and ability; 3) and then move on—emotionally and consciously. With the NCLEX, you cannot go back to any previous questions and change your answer, so forget about the question once you move past it. If you feel split between two options, pick the option that your gut instinct is telling you. Trust your gut instinct. You may not know exactly why, but something deep inside your consciousness is telling you the answer. Also, it is wiser to keep going through the exam than to sit and worry over a single question. Worrying and obsessing over a question you didn’t know will only distract your focus from the rest of the exam.

Answer:

Body System  Interventions to perform
Cardiovascular X Obtain a CBC.
X Assess distal pulses in all extremities.
X Elevate affected extremity.
Neurological  Assess sensation in left lower extremity.
 Initiate seizure precautions.
X Ask client to move toes on both feet.
Genitourinary  Strict I&O measurements.
 Administer diuretics for swelling.
X Obtain a pregnancy test.

Rationale: The client is being prepared for surgery. It is important to obtain preoperative laboratory data such as a CBC for a baseline measurement and to make sure that the client can tolerate the surgery. When a client has a severe fracture like this, it is very important to assess pulses, skin temperature, movement, and sensation in the extremity. A loss of pulses in the extremity could indicate compartment syndrome which is a medical emergency. Elevating the extremity can reduce swelling and decrease pain. Asking the patient to move her toes in both feet will help to assess neurological function in the extremities. A pregnancy test should be obtained for all females of child-bearing age before surgery to avoid any complications to the fetus. Assessing the sensation should be focused in the right extremity (the side of injury), not the left. There is no indication in the scenario that seizure precautions are warranted for this client. Strict I&Os are not needed at this time. The swelling this patient is experiencing in the extremity is related to inflammation due to injury; administering a diuretic will not resolve this issue and could create other unnecessary concerns.
