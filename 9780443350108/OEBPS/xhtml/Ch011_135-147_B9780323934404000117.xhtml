Mental Health Nursing

Important Definitions

  • • Mental health: Successful adjustment to changing internal and external environments. Achieving mental health (and sustaining it) is a lifelong process.
  • • Mental illness: Defines significant behavior that is the result of the client’s distress or disability.
    • — An inability to respond to the environment because of mental or medical conditions that affect the client’s thinking, ability to relate to others, mood, feeling, and daily functioning (e.g., impaired thinking, poor impulse control, or altered behavior)
    • — In many clients, it is a temporary loss, while in others it may be a lifelong series of intermittent exacerbations of illness
  • • The nurse-client relationship: a therapeutic relationship in which the nurse’s goal is to facilitate quality client-centered care by using nonverbal and verbal communication to build trust, provide comfort, and encourage change.
  • • Privacy and confidentiality: The client’s reasonable expectation is that information revealed to the nurse will not be disclosed to others. However, the nurse must explain to the client that information relevant to the individual’s treatment plan must be shared with the other members of the treatment team, especially if the client has thoughts of harm to self or others.

Therapeutic Communication

  • • Both verbal and nonverbal expression
  • • Goals based on client’s ability to participate
  • • Basic principles can be applied to all clients.
  • • Boundaries are established between the nurse and client.
    • — Principles
      • • Establish trust
      • • Demonstrate a nonjudgmental attitude
      • • Offer self; be empathetic, not sympathetic
      • • Use active listening
      • • Accept and support client’s feelings
      • • Clarify and validate client’s statements
      • • Use matter-of-fact approach
    • — Examples of therapeutic communication
      • • Silence: sit quietly and wait.
      • • Active listening: give full attention to the client.
      • • Open-ended questioning: promotes sharing
      • • Empathizing: demonstrates warmth and acknowledgment of feelings
      • • Restating: repeat what the client says to show understanding and to review what was said.

Coping and Defense Mechanisms

  • • Unconscious efforts to reduce anxiety
  • • Can be constructive or destructive.
  • • Coping is related to problem solving.
  • • Defense is related to protecting oneself.
    • — Denial
    • — Displacement
    • — Identification

Therapeutic Treatment Modalities

  • • Milieu therapy: physical, therapeutic, and social environment in which the client is receiving treatment
  • • Collaborative care: care that involves all members of the health care team; the team includes nurses, psychiatrists, social workers, mental health technicians, drug/alcohol therapists, etc.
  • • Interpersonal psychotherapy: uses a therapeutic relationship to modify the client’s feelings, attitudes, and behaviors
  • • Behavior Therapy: can take many forms and is used to help clients change behaviors
  • • Cognitive Therapy: adirective, time-limited approach
  • • Crisis Intervention: directed at resolution of the immediate crisis and to returning the individual to the precrisis level of functioning
  • • Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT): infrequently used treatment that uses electrically induced seizures to treat severely depressed individuals who do not respond to antidepressant medications and/or therapy

Group Therapy

  • • Involves a therapist and five to eight members (Fig. 11.1).
  • • Interactions among members provide feedback and support for the group and individual goals.
  • • Group Therapy Models
    • — Interpersonal group therapy
    • —  Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)
    • —   Self-help or support groups
      • • Stress management
      • • Self-care
      • • Medication education
      • • Dual diagnosis
    • — Family therapy
      • • Family member with the presenting symptoms indicates the presence of problems in the entire family
      • • A change in one member will bring about changes in the others
  • • Therapeutic Factors
    • —  Instillation of hope and optimism about group treatment
    • — Universality: members realize they are not alone with their problems, feelings, or thoughts
    • —  Imparting of information: members receive formal teaching by the leader or advice from peers
    • —   Altruism: members feel a reward from giving support to others
    • —  Recapitulation: members repeat patterns of behavior in the group that they learned in their families; feedback from the leader and peers provides opportunities to learn about their behavior
    • —  Development of socializing techniques: members learn new social skills based on feedback from others
    • — Imitative behavior: members copy behavior from the leaders or peers and can adopt healthier habits
    • — Interpersonal learning: members gain insight into themselves based on feedback from others; this occurs later in a group after establishing trust
    • — Group cohesiveness: in a mature group, members accept positive feedback and constructive criticism
    • — Catharsis: intense feelings, as judged by the member, are shared
    • — Existential resolution: members learn to accept painful aspects of life (i.e., loneliness, death) that affect everyone
  • • Stages of Group Development
    • — Initial stage: superficial communication; nurse and client orient to parameters of a therapeutic relationship.
    • — Working stage: real work is done by group.
    • — Termination stage: provides opportunity to learn to deal with letting go.

Anxiety

  • • Mild or moderate anxiety is a normal, subjective experience that includes feelings of apprehension, uneasiness, uncertainty, or dread.
  • • Severe stress may create levels of anxiety leading to depression, helplessness, and hopelessness that can end in panic.
  • • Types of Anxiety
    • — Mild: tension of everyday life
    • — Moderate: immediate concerns
    • — Severe: feeling that something bad is about to happen
    • — Panic: terror and a sense of impending doom
  • • Anxiety Disorders
    • — Generalized anxiety disorder: un-realistic anxiety about everyday worries occurring over a long time period
    • — Panic disorder: produces sudden feelings of intense apprehension, fear, or worry
    • — Phobia: irrational fear of an object, activity, or situation (e.g., agoraphobia)
      • • Client may recognize fear as unreasonable
      • • Associated with panic-level anxiety
      • • Defense mechanisms include repression and displacement
    • — Obsessive Compulsive Disorder: Anxious or fearful; Recurrent, intrusive thoughts; Uses Repetitive behaviors to decrease anxiety (e.g., compulsive handwashing)
    • — Nursing and collaborative management
      • • Reduce stimuli in environment
      • • Provide a calm, quiet environment
      • • Administer antianxiety medications as prescribed
      • • Administer selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs) as prescribed.

Somatic Symptom Disorder

  • • Types of somatic symptom disorders:
    • — Illness anxiety disorder or somatic disorder is a preoccupation with complaints about chronic symptoms that cause significant distress
      • • Persistent worry or complaints regarding physical illness without physical findings
    • — Factitious disorders are self-imposed fabricated symptoms; formerly called Munchausen syndrome.
    • — Factitious disorder imposed on another (FDIA) occurs when symptoms are imposed on another, e.g., parent induces illness in child (formerly Munchausen by proxy [MSP])
  • • Examples of somatoform disorders:
    • — Conversion disorder
    • — Hypochondriasis
    • — Somatization disorders
    • Treatment: Cognitive-behavioral therapy

Crisis Intervention

  • • Crisis
    • — A crisis results from experiencing a significant traumatic event or situation that the client cannot remedy by the use of the client’s available coping strategies (Fig. 11.2)
  • • Risk Factors
    • — Multiple comorbidities and losses
    • — Unexpected life changes
    • — Limited coping skills
    • — Chronic pain or disability
    • — Poor social support
    • — Concurrent psychiatric disorders, substance abuse, or disability
    • — Limited access to health care service
  • • Goal
    • — The goal is to return the client to precrisis level of functioning
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Assess for suicidal or homicidal thoughts or plans
    • — Help client feel safe and less anxious
    • — Listen carefully
    • — Be directive (e.g., nurse may arrange for shelter or contact a social worker)
    • — Mobilize social support
    • — Involve client in identifying realistic, acceptable interventions
    • — Plan regular follow-up

Posttraumatic Stress Disorder

  • • Re-experience of traumatic event involving (or real threat of) death, serious injury, or physical violation
  • • Recurrent and intrusive dreams or flashbacks
  • • Associated with four clusters:
    • — Avoidance of reminders
    • — Symptoms of numbing
    • — Alterations in behavior, including self-destructive (suicide), aggression, and irritability
    • — Persistent negative mood and alterations in cognition

Continuation of Stress and Anxiety

  • • Other Stressor-Related Disorders
    • — Anxiety displayed in symptoms
      • • Intrusive thoughts
      • • Nightmares
      • • Emotional detachment
      • • Flashbacks
  • • Reactions may be triggered by visible reminders of the trauma (e.g., physical disabilities).
  • • Responses include shock, anger, denial or panic, or self-destructive behavior (e.g., substance abuse or suicidal ideation).
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Implement suicidal and homicidal precautions as needed
    • — Consistently provide nonthreatening environment
    • — Actively listen to client’s description of events to identify the most troubling aspects
    • — Assist the client in identifying past successes to promote client’s ability to regain control

Dissociative Disorders

  • • An unconscious defense mechanism that protects the person against overwhelming anxiety (rarely encountered)
    • — Dissociative amnesia: one or more episodes of inability to recall personal information, usually due to trauma
    • — Dissociative identity disorder: two or more distinct identities, at least two of which recurrently take control
    • — Depersonalization/derealization disorder: persistent or recurrent episodes of feelings of detachment from oneself

Personality Disorders

  • • Individuals diagnosed with personality disorders have difficulty in work and personal relationships.
    • — Insensitive to the needs of others
    • — Demanding and fault-finding
    • — Have inability to trust others
    • — Blur boundaries between themselves and others
    • — Lack individual accountability
    • — Have poor impulse control: engage in self-damaging behaviors (impulsive sexual or suicidal behavior)
  • • Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder
    • — Obsession
      • • Persistently intrusive thoughts
    • — Compulsion
      • • Repetitive behaviors designed to divert unacceptable thought and reduce anxiety (e.g., compulsive handwashing)
  • • Antisocial personality disorder
    • — Aggressive toward others without remorse
    • — Is manipulative, self-centered, and impulsive
  • • Borderline personality disorder
    • — Highly changeable and intense affect
    • — Poor impulse control, engages in self-damaging behavior (e.g., sexual or suicidal)
    • — Pits perceived good person (nurse, friend, family member) against perceived bad person; switches which person is good
    • — Inflexible maladaptive behavior patterns
    • — In touch with reality
    • — Lacks insight into behavior
    • — Poor impulse control
    • — Forms of acting out include:
      • • Yelling and swearing
      • • Cutting own skin
      • • Manipulation
      • • Substance abuse
      • • Promiscuous sexual behaviors
      • • Suicide attempts
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Multiple psychotherapies are required to aid behavior change and promote satisfactory relationships
    • — Be alert to splitting and suicidal ideation
    • — Pharmacological intervention compromises a broad range, including antianxiety agents, anti-depressants, and antipsychotic drugs

Eating Disorders

  • • Compulsive Overeating
    • — Bingelike overeating without purging
    • — Lack of control over food consumption
  • • Anorexia Nervosa (Fig 11.3)

    • — The client experiences an altered body image
  • • Bulimia Nervosa (Fig. 11.3)
    • — Binge-purge syndrome: eating binges followed by purging behaviors

Depressive Disorders

  • • Depressive disorders are characterized by feelings of hopelessness and low self-esteem, as well as a tendency to blame self for every negative event.
  • • Twenty-five percent of those with depression have suicidal ideation.
  • • Behavior therapy, CBT, and interpersonal psychotherapy all reduce symptoms of depression and maintain their effects well after treatment has ended.
  • • Major Depressive Disorder
    • — Dysthymia (persistent depressive disorder)
    • — Premenstrual dysphoric disorder
    • — Substance/medication-induced depressive disorder
    • — Depressive disorder due to another medical condition

Suicide

  • • A suicide threat is a warning, direct or indirect, verbal or nonverbal, that a person is planning to take his or her own life.
  • • The person may give away prized possessions, make a will or funeral arrangements, or withdraw from friendships and social activities.
  • • Assessment includes whether the person has made a specific plan and whether the means to carry out the plan are available.
  • • Treatment:
    • — The highest priority is to protect the client from inflicting self-harm. Be vigilant, supervise medication administration, implement strategies to increase self-esteem, and increase social support.
    • — Never leave a suicidal client alone!
  • • Suicide Precautions
    • — Ongoing observations and monitoring of the client, direct visualization of client at all times
    • — Provide protective environment
    • — Search for and remove all weapons or potential weapons
    • — Be aware of major warning signs of an impending attempt
      • • — Client suddenly becomes “better” or “happy”

Bipolar Disorder

  • •   
    • Characterized by alternating episodes of mania and depression, between which the client has periods of normal mood and activity.
    • Periods of normal functioning may alternate with periods of illness (highs, lows, or a combination of both).
    • Many individuals continue to experience chronic interpersonal or occupational difficulties even during remission.
    • The mortality rate for bipolar disorder is severe. Suicide accounts for 5% of deaths among women and 10% of deaths among men with bipolar disorder compared with 1% and 2% in the general population.

Antidepressant Medications

  • • Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs)
    • — Action: inhibit serotonin reuptake
    • — Side effects/considerations: drowsiness, dizziness, headache, insomnia, depressed appetite
      • • Citalopram (Celexa)
      • • Escitalopram (Lexapro)
      • • Fluvoxamine (Luvox)
      • • Fluoxetine (Prozac)
      • • Paroxetine (Paxil)
      • • Sertraline (Zoloft)
  • • Serotonin Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs)
    • — Action: block reabsorption of serotonin and norepinephrine
      • • May also have therapeutic effects on neuropathic pain
      • • Venlafaxine (Effexor)
      • • Desvenlafaxine (Pristiq)
      • • Duloxetine (Cymbalta)
      • • Levomilnacipran (Fetzima)
  • • Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
    • — Action: block reuptake of norepinephrine (and serotonin) at presynaptic neurons
    • — Side effects/considerations: may take 2–4 weeks to produce effect; tachycardia, dizziness, insomnia, drowsiness, fluid retention. Note: Trazodone can cause priapism. Priapism affects the penis and, in rare instances, the clitoris.
      • • Amitriptyline
      • • Clomipramine (Anafranil)
      • • Desipramine (Norpramin)
      • • Imipramine (Tofranil)
      • • Nortriptyline
      • • Protriptyline (Vivactil)
      • • Trimipramine (Surmontil)
  • • Atypical Antidepressants
    • — Bupropion (Wellbutrin) and bupropion SR (Wellbutrin SR)

HIGH-ALERT Antidepressant Use

  • • Serotonin syndrome
    • — Hyperthermia, rigidity, cognitive impairments, and autonomic symptoms
    • — Potentially fatal and may occur at any time during therapy with SSRIs or SNRIs combined with MAOIs
    • — Treatment is symptomatic with propranolol, cooling blankets, chlorpromazine for hyperthermia, diazepam for muscle rigidity or rigors
  • • Antidepressant apathy: some clients lose interest in life and the events around them (related to SSRIs)
  • • Antidepressant withdrawal syndrome: abrupt cessation of antidepressants engenders withdrawal symptoms
  • • Antidepressant loss of effectiveness: sometimes medications are no longer effective
  • • Antidepressant-induced suicide: especially 18- to 24-year-olds in the early stages of treatment
  • • Lithium toxicity is potentially fatal:
    • —  Lithium level range: 0.8–1.2 mEq/L (0.8–1.2 mmol/L)
    • —  Expected side effects: fine hand tremor, polyuria, mild thirst, weight gain, mild nausea
    • —  Signs of lithium toxicity (level >1.5 mEq/L or >1.5 mmol/L): N/V, diarrhea, slurred speech, muscle weakness. Withhold medication; obtain lithium blood level
    • —  Advanced toxicity: coarse hand tremor, persistent gastrointestinal (GI) upset, mental confusion, muscle hyperirritability
    • —  Severe toxicity: hyperthermia as high as 108 °F (42.2°C), cognitive impairments, autonomic symptoms, ataxia, blurred vision, clonic movements, seizures, severe hypotension, cardiac dysrhythmias, death
    • —  Nursing and collaborative management
      • • There is no known antidote for lithium poisoning
      • • Immediately discontinue lithium and notify HCP.
      • • Client may need to transfer to a critical care unit

Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs)

  • • Use caution when administering MAOIs.
  • • Inhibit the enzyme monoamine oxidase, which is present in the brain, blood platelets, liver, spleen, and kidneys.
  • • Administered to clients with depression who have not responded to other antidepressant therapies, including electroconvulsive therapy (ECT).
  • • There must be a 2-week wash-out period between using MAOIs and SSRIs or SNRIs (in either direction, i.e., from MAOI to SSRI, or vice versa); otherwise, the mixture can trigger a hypertensive crisis.
  • • A hypertensive crisis, or hypotension, coma, or seizures may occur with concurrent use of:
    • — Tyramine-containing foods (red wine, aged cheese, raisins)
    • — Decongestants, amphetamines
    • — Antidepressants, dopamine
    • — Opioids epinephrine, guanethidine
    • — Levodopa, methyldopa
    • — Nasal norepinephrine
    • — Reserpine, vasoconstrictors
  • • Concurrent use with opioid analgesics may cause hypertension or hypotension, coma, or seizures.

Schizophrenia Spectrum and Other Psychotic Disorders

  • • Group of mental disorders characterized by psychotic features.
    • — Negative symptoms
    • Anhedonia: a reduced ability or inability to experience pleasure in everyday life
    • Avolition: loss of motivation; difficulty beginning and sustaining goal-directed activities; reduction in motivation or goal-directed behavior
    • Asociality: decreased desire for, or comfort during, social interaction
    • Affective blunting: reduced or constricted affect
    • Apathy: a decreased interest in, or attention to, activities or beliefs that would otherwise be interesting or important
    • Alogia: reduction in speech, sometimes called poverty of speech
    • — Positive symptoms
    • Delusions: false beliefs
    • Delusions of persecution
    • Delusions of grandeur
    • Somatic delusions
    • Perceptual delusions
  • • Perceptual distortions
    • Difficulty understanding or using information
  • • Illusions
    • Brief experiences of misperception of reality that usually occur with delirium, delirium tremens (DTs), or are drug induced
  • • Hallucinations
    • Perceptions without a basis in reality
    • Includes five senses (smell, touch, etc.)
  • • Movement disorders, flat affect, poverty of speech
  • • Difficulty understanding or using information.
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Safety is the priority!
    • — Assess for risk of violence to self or others and take appropriate precautions
    • — Provide quiet, soothing environment
    • — Establish routine and boundaries
    • — Provide stable, nonthreatening, brief, social inter-actions
    • — If client is acting frightened or scared, increase physical space surrounding client and approach client calmly
    • — Encourage reality-based interests
  • • Traditional Medications
    • — Chlorpromazine
    • — Haloperidol
    • — Nursing Assessment
      • • Screen for EPS using Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale (AIMS)
      • • Encourage fluid, gum, and hard candy, and increase fiber intake
    • Long-Acting Medications
    • — Clozapine (Clozaril)
    • — Risperdal (risperidone) long-acting injection
    • — Side effects
      • • Blood dyscrasias and neuroleptic malignant syndrome (very high fever)
    • — Nursing and collaborative management
      • • Reinforce teaching client to change positions slowly to prevent dizziness, and report urinary retention
      • • Stress importance of maintaining medication therapy
  • • Atypical Medications
    • — Aripiprazole (Abilify)
      • • Purpose: Treat all positive and negative symptoms
      • • Side effects: Multiple side effects depending on medication
      • • Nursing assessment: Tolerance to effects, neuro-leptic malignant syndrome (NMS)

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

  • • Involves autonomic, motor, and behavioral symptoms.
  • • Antipsychotic is stopped immediately if the client develops signs of NMS.
    • — Severe muscle rigidity
    • — Increased temperature (fever >100.4°F [38°C)] as high as 102 to 104°F [38.8 to 40°C]), increased pulse, and labile blood pressure
    • — Confusion (altered mental status)
    • — Autonomic dysfunction
    • — Agitation
    • — Involves autonomic, motor, and behavioral symptoms
  • • Associated medications
    • — Haloperidol, fluphenazine, trifluoperazine, thioridazine, loxapine, clozaril, risperidone, olanzapine, ziprasidone, reserpine, droperidol, lithium, levodopa, desipramine, amantadine

Substance Abuse Disorders

  • • Substance dependence: a pattern of repeated use of a substance
  • • Substance tolerance: a need for more of the substance to reach the desired effect.
  • • Substance abuse: recurrent use of a substance
  • • Substance withdrawal: symptoms occur when blood levels of a substance decline.

Opioid Abuse

  • • Opioids are a type of medicine often used to help relieve pain. Signs and symptoms of abuse include:
    • — Poor coordination
    • — Drowsiness
    • — Shallow or slow breathing rate
    • — Nausea, vomiting
    • — Constipation
    • — Physical agitation
    • — Poor decision making
    • — Slurred speech
    • — Mood swings
    • — Euphoria
    • — Irritability
    • — Depression
    • — Anxiety attacks
  • • An overdose of opioids requires immediate emergency medical treatment (naloxone).

Alcohol Abuse

  • • Alcohol is a CNS depressant.
  • • Physical dependence: biological need for alcohol to avoid physical withdrawal symptoms.
  • • Psychological dependence: craving for the subjective effect of alcohol.
  • • Intoxication: blood alcohol of 0.1% (100 mg [21.7 mmol/L] of alcohol per deciliter of blood) or greater.
  • • Clients may present to the emergency department with blood alcohol levels as high as 0.3% to 0.4% (300 to 400 mg/dL or 65.1–86.62 mmol/L). 0.4% is potentially fatal.
  • • Disulfiram (Antabuse) Therapy
    • — Alcohol deterrent
    • — Reinforce teaching to the client on disulfiram therapy to avoid the use of substances that contain alcohol (e.g., cough medicines, mouthwashes, and aftershave lotions)
  • • Other medications used to assist with cravings:
    • — Acamprosate calcium
    • — Naltrexone
  • • Alcohol Withdrawal
    • — At the time of admission, always ask client when their last drink was ingested
    • — Peaks after 24 to 48 hours
    • — Chlordiazepoxide (Librium) is the most commonly prescribed medication for acute alcohol withdrawal.
  • • Withdrawal Delirium
    • — Peaks 48 to 72 hours after cessation of intake and lasts 2 to 3 days
      • • It is a medical emergency. Notify registered nurse (RN).
      • • Death can occur from myocardial infarction, fat emboli, peripheral vascular collapse, electrolyte imbalance, aspiration pneumonia, or suicide.

Neurocognitive Disorders (NCDs): Autism Spectrum Disorder

  • • Autism Spectrum Disorder: a group of developmental disorders that includes autism, Asperger disorder, childhood disintegrative disorder, and pervasive developmental disorder not otherwise specified.
  • • — Prognosis: No known cure. Language skills and intellectual level are the strongest factors related to prognosis. A small percentage of individuals with the disorder go on to live and work independently as adults.
  • • — Etiology: no known cause.
  • • — Clinical Description (Fig. 11.4)
    • Hyperactivity
    • Short attention span
    • Impulsivity
    • Aggressive behavior
    • Self-injurious behavior
    • Temper tantrums
    • Repetitive mannerisms
    • Preoccupation with objects
    • Spoken language is often absent
    • “Islands of genius” (savant-like syndrome)

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

  • • Etiology: no known cause, but there is a strong correlation between genetic factors and ADHD
  • • Clinical Description
    • — Fidgeting when sitting
    • — Gets up when expected to stay seated
    • — Excessive running when it is dangerous or inappropriate
    • — Loud and disruptive play during quiet activities
    • — Forgets and misses appointments
    • — Fails to meet deadlines
    • — Loses the train of conversation
    • — Changes topics inappropriately
    • — Does not follow rules of games
  • • Treatment with CNS stimulants may be required to reduce hyperactive behavior and lengthen attention span.
  • • Prognosis
    • — Continues into adolescence in the majority of children
    • — Many adults with ADHD in childhood report a decrease of hyperactivity but a continuation of difficulty concentrating or attending to complex projects.

Neurocognitive Disorders (NCD) (Not Classified as Mental Illness)

  • • Major and Mild NCDs
    • — Primarily acquired cognitive symptoms that represent a decline
    • — Typically refers to degeneration of younger clients caused by nerve cell deterioration (e.g., traumatic brain injury, HIV)
    • — Symptoms
      • • Significant cognitive decline
      • • Interference with independent functioning
      • • Not caused by other mental disorder
  • • Mild NCD
    • — Does not interfere with independence; not due to delirium
    • — Impairment in judgment, abstract thinking, problem-solving ability, and behavior may also be present
    • — Providing a safe environment is the priority when cognitive domains are affected
    • — Cognitive domains
      • • Learning and memory
      • • Language
      • • Perceptual-motor
      • • Social cognition
      • • Executive function
      • • Complex attention
  • • Dementia: syndrome of progressive deterioration in intellectual functioning secondary to structural or functional changes usually associated with aging
    • — It is not a mental health disorder, per the DSM-5
    • — Marked by long-term and short-term memory loss
    • — Caused by nerve cell deterioration
    • — Impairment in judgment, abstract thinking, problem solving ability, and behavior
    • — Providing safe environment is the priority
  • • Alzheimer’s disease
    • — Irreversible form of senile dementia caused by nerve cell deterioration
    • — The priority is client safety
    • — Maintain comfort, toilet as necessary; keep dry
    • — Reduce environmental stimulation during late afternoon and evening
    • — Maintain daily routine
    • — Provide environmental cues; turn on lights before dusk; provide night-light
    • — Provide soothing music
    • — Provide reassurance and companionship, especially during difficult evening period (Fig. 11.5)

Test-Taking Strategy

In the NCLEX world (while you are testing), there are some special considerations that you should be aware of. For instance, as the tester, you can assume that you are only caring for this one client that is being presented in the scenario or each individual question. The NCLEX does not account for normal everyday distractions that nurses must deal with in their daily routines. As the nurse in the scenario, you can also assume that for any options that are listed, you have an order from the doctor already written that will allow you to perform that task. That means, don’t say to yourself that you would not pass that medication or give that IV fluid bolus (for instance) because the doctor would have to order it. You can safely assume that the options are orders from the physician. Finally, only call the physician when something occurs that is not an expected finding for the disease process. Therefore, you must know the difference between signs and symptoms that are normal to see in that particular disease process and those that are indicating a life-threatening potential complication of the disease process.

Highlight negative symptoms of schizophrenia the client is experiencing.

Answer:

Client history: He has been working at a local electronics store and lives at home with his parents while attending a local community college. Parents report he has always been a good student and is socially active. Last semester his grades began declining and he has frequently been skipping classes. He has become very withdrawn and spends most of his time alone in his room. His grooming has deteriorated; often will go days without showering. For several weeks prior to this admission, he has insisted on keeping all of the curtains in the house closed. For the past 2 days he has refused to eat, saying, “They are poisoning me.” Upon approach, it is noted that he appears to be talking to someone, but no one is there. When spoken to, he looks around and answers in a whisper but gives little information. He states, “They are watching me and told me not to cooperate.”

Rationale: Negative symptoms of schizophrenia account for a large portion of the long-term disability and poor functional outcomes in clients with the disorder and refer to a decreased or absence of motivation and interest, or verbal and emotional expression. Negative symptoms include: anhedonia, avolition, asociality, affective blunting, apathy, and alogia. The highlighted items are examples of these negative symptom types. Positive symptoms include: delusions, perceptual distortions, illusions, and hallucinations.
