Advanced Clinical Concepts and Disaster Management

Acute Conditions: Shock

  • • Stages of Shock (Fig. 4.1)
    • — Initial Stage
      • • Decrease in mean arterial pressure (MAP) of 5 to 10 mm Hg from baseline value
      • • Increased sympathetic stimulation
      • • Mild vasoconstriction
      • • Increased heart rate (HR)
    • — Compensatory Stage
      • • Decrease in MAP of 10 to 15 mm Hg from baseline value
      • • Continued sympathetic stimulation
      • • Continued sympathetic stimulation results in systemic vascular constriction
      • • Increased HR
      • • Decreased pulse pressure
      • • Chemical compensation
      • • Renin, aldosterone, and antidiuretic hormone secretion produces an increased vasoconstriction (compensates for shock)
      • • Decreased urine output
      • • Stimulation of the thirst reflex
      • • Some anaerobic metabolism in nonvital organs
      • • Mild acidosis
      • • Mild hyperkalemia
    • — Progressive Stage
      • • Decrease in MAP of >20 mm Hg from baseline value
      • • Anoxia of nonvital organs
      • • Hypoxia of vital organs
      • • Overall metabolism is anaerobic
      • • Moderate acidosis
      • • Moderate hyperkalemia
      • • Tissue ischemia
    • — Refractory Stage
      • • Severe tissue hypoxia with ischemia and necrosis
      • • Release of myocardial depressant factor from the pancreas
      • • Buildup of toxic metabolites
      • • Multiple organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS)
      • • Death
  • • Types of Shock
    • — Hypovolemic
      • • ↓ Circulating volume related to internal or external blood loss or dehydration
      • • Older adults at risk related to diuretic therapy, ↓ thirst reflex, anticoagulation therapy
    • — Vasogenic (Anaphylactic, Neurogenic, Septic)
      • • Excessive vasodilation and impaired distribution of blood flow
      • • Older adult risk related to ↓ immune response, malignancies, malnutrition
    • — Cardiogenic
      • • Pump failure
      • • Results in ↓ cardiac output and MAP
      • • Older adult risk related to diabetes mellitus, cardiomyopathies, cognitive impairment
      • • Myocardial infarction (MI) is the most common cause
    • — Obstructive
      • • Physical obstruction that impedes filling and pumping of the heart
      • • Pericarditis, cardiac tamponade
      • • Older adult risk related to autoimmune disorders, malignancies, pulmonary hypertension
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Correct decreased tissue perfusion and restore cardiac output.
      • • Optimize oxygenation and ventilation.
      • • Fluid resuscitation
      • • Correct positioning, monitoring, registered nurse (RN) collaboration
    • — In cardiogenic shock, volume expanders may precipitate pulmonary edema.

Treatment of Shock

  • • — Drug therapy
    • Restore cardiac function based on effect of shock on preload, afterload, and contractility
    • Replace blood volume or fluid loss
    • Administer medications depending on cause of shock
  • • Vasodilators
  • • Vasoconstrictors
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Data collection
      • • Vital signs
      • • Mental status
      • • Urine output

Continuum of Sepsis

  • • Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS)
  • • Sepsis
  • • Severe sepsis
  • • Septic shock
  • • MODS

Sepsis

  • • SIRS is an assortment of insults, including sepsis, ischemia, infarction, and injury.
  • • Generalized inflammation occurs in organs remote from the initial insult.
  • • Syndrome starts with an infection that progresses to bacteremia, then sepsis, then severe sepsis, then septic shock, and finally MODS.
  • • The prognosis for MODS is poor.
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • —The most important goal is to prevent the progression of SIRS to MODS.
    • —The nursing role is attentive data collection and ongoing monitoring to detect early signs of organ dysfunction.
    • — Collaborative care focuses on:
      • • Prevention strategies
      • • Early recognition of subtle changes in HR, systolic BP, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, urinary output, and central nervous system changes
      • • Serum lactate and glucose levels
      • • Blood culture before antibiotics (Fig. 4.2)
      • • Broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 to 3 hours of admission
      • • Maintenance of tissue oxygenation
      • • Nutritional and metabolic support
      • • Support of individual failing organs

Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)

  • • DIC is a serious homeostasis disorder resulting from overstimulation of clotting factors followed by anticlotting processes in response to diseases or injury.
  • • Astute ongoing evaluation and assessment is needed.
  • • Faster clotting + decrease in clotting factors and platelets + hemorrhage = DIC
  • • DIC may lead to uncontrollable hemorrhage.
  • • Early detection of occult and overt bleeding is the primary goal.
  • • D-dimer assay measures the degree of fibrinolysis (fibrin products in the blood).
  • • Assess the client for signs of external and internal bleeding.
  • • Be alert for manifestations of the syndrome.
  • • Appropriate treatment measures can be challenging and sometimes paradoxical.
  • • Heparin infusion (early in DIC, when clots are forming)
  • • Blood, fresh frozen plasma (FFP) transfusions, and cryoprecipitate

Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)

  • • Is a severe form of respiratory failure
    • — Is considered to be present if the client has hypoxemia that does not improve with oxygen
    • — A predisposing condition for ARDS within 48 hours of clinical manifestations
    • — New interstitial or alveolar infiltrates on a chest x-ray (whiteout or white lung)
  • • Progression
    • — Is marked by alveolar capillary membrane damage with subsequent leakage of fluids into the interstitial spaces and alveoli
    • — As ARDS progresses, profound respiratory distress develops, requiring endotracheal intubation and positive-pressure ventilation.
  • • Nursing Assessment
    • — Hypoxemia
    • — Dyspnea
    • — Scattered crackles
    • — Increased work of breathing
    • — Intercostal retractions
    • — Respiratory alkalosis (early) leads to respiratory acidosis (later)
    • — Pleural effusions
    • — Decreased cardiac output
    • — Cyanosis
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Overall goals
      • • PaO2 of at least 60 mm Hg
      • • Adequate lung ventilation to maintain normal pH
    • — Goals for recovering client
      • • PaO2 within normal limits for age or baseline values on room air
      • • SaO2 >90%
      • • Patent airway
      • • Clear lungs on auscultation

Delirium

  • • An acute state of confusion, and difficulty concentrating (Fig. 4.3)
    • — Common among elderly, hospitalized clients
    • — May indicate an impending change in condition (i.e., sepsis, urinary tract infection [UTI])
  • • Risk Factors
    • — Sleep deprivation, advanced age, or vision and hearing impairment
    • — Use of opioids or corticosteroids
    • — Drug or alcohol abuse
    • — UTI; fluid and electrolyte imbalance
    • — Postoperative (unscheduled surgery), intensive care unit, or emergent delirium
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Prevention and early recognition (monitor neurological status)
    • — Notify RN or health care provider (HCP) of any changes
    • — Protect the client from harm.
    • — Provide a low-stimulation environment (calm).
    • —  Provide the appropriate level of supervision/surveillance.
    • — Reorient the client and communicate with simple statements
    • — Consider management with neuroleptic drugs (e.g., haloperidol [Haldol]) as prescribed
    • — Encourage family visibility and support

Basic Life Support

  • • Cardiac arrest is the most common event requiring cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR).
  • • CAB—Chest compressions–Airway–Breathing
    • — High-quality chest compressions
    • — Push hard and push fast
    • — Adult 100 to 120 compressions/min
  • • In Hospital Cardiac Arrest
    • — Determine unresponsiveness
    • — Activate emergency response or cardiac arrest team
    • — Call for automated external defibrillator (AED) or emergency crash cart (do not leave client)
    • — Initiate CPR with basic cardiac life support (BCLS) guidelines
    • — Initiate compressions (Fig. 4.4)
      • • After 30 compressions, open airway with head tilt–chin lift (Fig. 4.5) and ventilate with bag-valve mask (provide two breaths, each over 1 second)
      • • Maintain compressions to breaths ratio of 30:2
    • —  Once the defibrillator or AED arrives, apply quick-look paddles or AED to determine whether defibrillation is necessary; defibrillate as indicated according to hospital policies and procedures
    • — Resume CPR

CPR and Choking Basics: Neonates and Children Ages 1 to 8

  • • Indications for CPR in children are different than those for adults.
    • —   Neonates and infants: hypoxia, hypoglycemia, hypothermia, acidosis, hypercoagulability
    • —    Children: respiratory arrest, prolonged hypoxemia secondary to respiratory insult or shock, including septic shock
    • —  If no response occurs, call a “code,” or cardiac arrest, to initiate response of cardiac arrest team
    • —  Obtain AED or emergency crash cart with defi-brillator.
  • • Guidelines vary based on the age of the child. If the collapse is witnessed:
    • —  If the infant or child is unresponsive, call a “code,” or cardiac arrest, to initiate response of cardiac arrest team
    • —  Obtain AED or emergency crash cart with defi-brillator
    • —  Begin CPR per guidelines listed below
  • • Guidelines vary based on the age of the child. If the collapse is not witnessed:
    • —  Check for pulse
      • • Infant aged <1 year: brachial pulse
      • • Children aged 1 year to puberty: carotid or femoral
    • — Compressions (begin within 10 seconds)
      • • Infants: Compressions cover one-third of the anterior/posterior diameter of the chest; depth is 1.5 inches (Fig. 4.6)
      • • Children: Compressions cover one-third of the anterior/posterior diameter of the chest; depth is 2 inches
      • • Compression to Breath Ratio: For infants and children, the ratio is 30:2 when only one rescuer is available. When two or more rescuers are available to assist with resuscitation, then the ratio changes to 15:2.
    • — Deliver each breath over 1 second and observe for gastric/abdominal distension
    • — If the infant or child does not respond after 2 minutes of high-quality CPR, then call a “code,” or cardiac arrest, to initiate response of cardiac arrest team
    • — Obtain AED or emergency crash cart with defi-brillator

Disaster Management

  • • The nurse is an active team member in the event of biological, chemical, radioactive, mass trauma, and natural disasters.
  • • The nurse plays a role at all three levels of disaster management (primary, secondary, tertiary).
  • • Levels of prevention in disaster management:
    • — Primary: planning, training, and educating personnel and the public (Fig. 4.7)
    • — Secondary: triage, treatment, shelter supervision
    • — Tertiary: follow-up, recovery assistance, prevention of future disasters

A closeup of five trauma team participants in scrubs attending to the patient on the table

Triage
  • • The goal of triage is to maximize the number of survivors by sorting the injured as treatable and untreatable, using the criteria of potential for survival and availability of resources.
  • • Color-coded system (in order of priority):
    • — Red: life threatening, need immediate intervention
    • —  Yellow: injuries with systemic effects and compli-cations
    • — Green: minor injuries, no systemic complications
    • — Black: dying or deceased—catastrophic injuries
  • • START (Simple Triage And Rapid Treatment) method.

Next Generation NCLEX® Examination (NGN)–Style Question

Multiple Response: Select All That Apply

Scenario: The PN is caring for a client who has a new prescription for warfarin 2 mg PO daily. The PN is providing medication education for the client and is discussing dietary instructions, including foods to avoid while taking this medication.

Question: Which vegetable(s) would the PN instruct this client to avoid while taking warfarin? (Select all that apply.)

  1. 1. Collard greens
  2. 2. Iceberg lettuce
  3. 3. Broccoli
  4. 4. Brussels sprouts
  5. 5. Green beans
  6. 6. Spinach
  7. 7. Zucchini squash
  8. 8. Kale

Answer: 1, 3, 4, 6, 8

Rationale: Collard greens, broccoli, brussels sprouts, spinach, and kale are vitamin K–rich foods and should be avoided while taking warfarin and other anticoagulants. Iceberg lettuce, green beans, and zucchini squash are lower in vitamin K and are good substitutes for these vegetables for a well-balanced diet.
