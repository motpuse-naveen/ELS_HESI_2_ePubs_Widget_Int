Chest Tubes and Water or Dry Seal Management

  • • Chest tubes are inserted into the pleural space to remove air/fluid and restore intrathoracic pressure, which allows the lung to reexpand.
  • • A chest collection drainage system has three compartments or chambers.
  • • Collection Chamber (Fig. 5.1)
    • — Air and fluid are collected from the pleural or mediastinal space.
    • — Fluid remains and air is vented to the second compartment, the water seal chamber.
  • • Water Seal Chamber (Fig. 5.2)
    • — The chamber contains 2 cm of water, which prevents backflow and acts as a one-way valve.
    • — Fluctuations in the water level are known as tidaling; fluid should move upward with each inspiration and downward with each expiration.
  • • Suction Control Chamber
    • — Wet suction uses 20 cm of water to aid in draining air/fluid from the chest.
    • — Dry suction provides effective level of vacuum by continuously balancing the forces of suction control and atmosphere.
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • —Monitor respiratory effort and pain levels.
    • — Check that water level is accurate at 2 cm every shift; add sterile water to chamber if needed.
    • —Keep the unit lower than client’s chest.
    • — Keep the unit straight and all tubing loosely coiled below chest level, with all connections tight and taped.
    • —Monitor the fluid drainage and mark the time of measurement and the fluid level; notify health care provider (HCP) if there is >70 mL/h drainage.
    • —Assess for tidaling; observe for air bubbling in the water seal chamber. (Excessive bubbling in the water-seal chamber may indicate an air leak.)
    • —Replace the unit when collection chamber is full.
    • —If chest tube is accidentally dislodged, then:
      • • Cover with a dry sterile dressing.
      • • If an air leak is noted, tape on three sides.
      • • Notify registered nurse (RN) or HCP immediately.

Pneumonia

  • • Inflammation of lung tissue, causing consolidation of exudate
  • • Etiology
    • — Bacterial (gram-negative is the most severe), viral, fungal (rare), or aspiration
    • — Community acquired (CAP) or hospital acquired (HAP)
    • — Ventilator associated event (VAE)
  • • Risk Factors
    • — Age >65 years or residents in long-term care
    • — Recent surgery (abdominal, thoracic)
    • —Altered consciousness: alcoholism, head injury, seizures, smoking, splenic dysfunction, anesthesia, drug overdose, cerebrovascular accident (CVA)
    • — Prolonged immobility
    • — Immunosuppression
  • • Prevention
    • —CDC recommends pneumococcal vaccination for all infants as a series of four doses by age 2.
    • —CDC recommends additional pneumococcal vaccination for children and adults with certain chronic medical conditions or other risk factors, and all adults over 65 years old. See CDC website for lists of these conditions.
    • — Influenza vaccine yearly
  • • VAE Practice Bundle
    • — Meticulous hand hygiene
    • — Head of bed (HOB) elevation 30–45 degrees
    • — Oral care per facility protocol
    • — Suction only as needed with sterile technique
    • — Use routine peptic ulcer prophylaxis
    • —Collaborate with RN for Spontaneous Awakening Trials (SAT) with Spontaneous Breathing Trials (SBT)
    • —Early mobilization protocols, venous thromboembolism (VTE) prophylaxis
  • • Nursing Assessment
    • — Tachypnea
    • — Productive cough
    • — Pleuritic pain
    • — Fever of abrupt onset
    • — Dyspnea
    • — Increased tactile fremitus
    • — Mental status changes
    • — Crackles, decreased breath sounds
    • — Dullness on percussion
    • — ABGs indicative of hypoxemia
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Handwashing to reduce cross-contamination
    • — Blood and sputum cultures, before antibiotic administration
    • — Isolation if prescribed
    • — Administer and encourage fluids if not contraindicated.
    • — Administer antipyretics.
    • — Administer empiric anti-infectives as prescribed.
    • — Manage pain.
    • — Monitor O2 saturation and administer humidified O2 as appropriate.
    • — Reinforce teaching on coughing, turning, and deep breathing techniques.
    • — Encourage use of incentive spirometry.
    • — Individualize rest and activity; mobility increases diaphragm movement, chest expansion, and secretion mobilization.
  • • Anti-infectives
    • — Penicillins

Chronic Airflow Limitation (CAL) (Fig. 5.3)

  • • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (Copd)
    • — Chronic progressive disease
    • — A collection of lower airway disorders that interfere with airflow and gas exchange, which includes:
      • • Emphysema
      • • Chronic bronchitis

An illustration of lungs with a set of enlarged views of bronchioles and alveoli shows causes of narrowed airways as follows: • In asthma, the airways overreact to common stimuli with bronchospasm, edematous swelling of the mucous membranes, and copious production of thick, tenacious mucus by abundant hypertrophied mucous glands. Airway obstruction is usually intermittent. • Centriacinar or centrilobular emphysema affects the respiratory bronchioles most severely. It is usually more severe in the upper lung. • In emphysema, lung proteases collapse the walls of bronchioles and alveolar air sacs. As these walls collapse, the bronchioles and alveoli transform from a number of small elastic structures with great air-exchanging surface area into fewer, larger, inelastic structures with little surface area. Air is trapped in these distal structures, especially during forced expiration, such as coughing, and the lungs hyperinflate. The trapped air stagnates and can no longer supply needed oxygen to the nearby capillaries. • In chronic bronchitis, infection or bronchial irritants cause increased secretions, edema, bronchospasm, and impaired mucociliary clearance. Inflammation of the bronchial walls causes them to thicken. This thickening, together with excessive mucus, blocks the airways and hinders gas exchange. • Panacinar or panlobular emphysema affects the entire acinar unit. It is usually more severe in the lower lung.

Pulmonary Tuberculosis (TB)

  • • TB is a communicable disease caused by the Mycobacterium tuberculosis or the tubercle bacillus, an acid-fast organism, spread by airborne transmission.
  • • Primary Infection (Fig. 5.5)
  • • — Bacteria inhaled
  • • Latent TB infection (LTBI): the TB bacteria remain inactive.
  • • Risk Factors
    • —Immunocompromised status, comorbidity in clients diagnosed with human immune deficiency virus (HIV)
    • —Multidrug-resistant TB
    • —Disproportionately in poor, underserved, and minority populations
    • —Institutionalized individuals
  • • Nursing Assessment
    • • — Low-grade fever
    • • — Pallor
    • • — Chills
    • • — Night sweats
    • • — Easy fatigability
    • • — Anorexia
    • • — Weight loss
  • Collaborative Management
    • • —  Diagnosis with nucleic acid amplification test (NAAT), QuantiFERON-TB, or purified protein derivative (PPD) (Mantoux test)
    • • — Notify public health department.
    • • — Airborne precautions isolation
    • • — Single-occupancy room with negative pressure
    • • — High-efficiency particulate air (HEPA) masks
    • • — Sputum specimens: Three negative in a row/cleared out isolation
    • • — Reinforce teaching client to cover the nose and mouth with paper tissues whenever coughing, sneezing, or producing sputum.
    • • — Emphasize handwashing after handling sputum and soiled tissues.
    • • — If the client needs to be out of the negative-pressure room, he or she must wear a standard isolation mask to prevent exposure to others.

An illustrative flowchart for primary tuberculosis infection with progression to secondary infection and active disease is as follows: • Aerosol transmission of the tuberculosis organism into the lungs of the person causes a tuberculosis lesion. • In 90% to 95% of infected individuals, the primary lesion, walled off by caseation, forms a scar that shows on chest x-ray. • In 5% to 10% of infected individuals, lesion is followed by progression to active tuberculosis. • The primary lesion is followed by secondary infection: reaction of tuberculosis after immunosuppression, HIV infection, or smoking. • Secondary infection is followed by progression to active tuberculosis. • In active tuberculosis cavities open into the bronchi, allowing spread of M. tuberculosis through coughing.

TB Drugs and Side Effects

  • • First-Line Drugs and Their Side Effects
    • — First-line drugs are bacteriocidal against rapidly dividing cells and against semi-dormant bacteria.
      • • Isoniazid (INH): clinical hepatitis, fulminant hepatitis, peripheral neurotoxicity
      • • Rifampin (Rifadin, Rofact): cutaneous reactions, gastrointestinal (GI) disturbance (nausea, anorexia, abdominal pain), flu-like syndrome, hepatotoxicity, immunological reactions, orange discoloration of bodily fluids (sputum, urine, sweat, tears)
      • • Ethambutol hydrochloride (Etibi): retrobulbar neuritis (decreased red-green color discrimination), skin rash
      • • Rifabutin (Mycobutin): hematologic toxicity, GI symptoms, polyarthralgia, pseudojaundice, orange discoloration of bodily fluids
      • • Pyrazinamide (PZA): hepatotoxicity, GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting), polyarthralgia, skin rash, hyperuricemia, dermatitis
  • • Managing Active TB
    • — 8 weeks with INH, rifampin, ethambutol, and PZA
    • — Followed by 18 weeks of INH and rifampin

Pulmonary Embolus

  • • Any substance can cause an embolism. Typically, the embolism enters the venous circulation and lodges in the pulmonary vasculature (Fig. 5.6).
    • —Blood embolism from deep vein thrombosis (DVT), large vein thrombosis (iliac or femoral), or atrial fibrillation
    • —Air embolism from intravenous therapy
    • —Fat embolism from long bone fractures
    • —Amniotic fluid embolism during pregnancy/childbirth (extremely rare)
  • • Risk factors for venous thromboembolism (VTE) leading to pulmonary embolism (PE)
    • — Prolonged immobility
    • — Central venous catheters
    • — Surgery in last 3 months
    • — Obesity
    • — Malignancy
    • — Clotting disorders
    • — History of thromboembolism
    • — Smoking, birth control pill, pregnancy
    • — Heart failure
  • • Signs and Symptoms
    • — Dyspnea, tachypnea, tachycardia
    • — Sharp, stabbing chest pain
    • — Cough
    • — Crackles, pleural friction rub
    • — ↓O2 saturation, respiratory alkalosis, and then respiratory acidosis
    • — Pleural friction rub
    • — S3 or S4 heart sound
    • — Feeling of impending doom
    • — Low-grade fever
    • — Hemoptysis
    • — Hypoxemia
    • — Elevated D-dimer level
    • — Diagnosed by findings: spiral computed tomography (CT), transesophageal echocardiography (TEE), Ventilation/Quality of perfusion (V/Q) scan
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Prevention
      • • Range-of-motion exercises
      • • Ambulate and turn.
      • • Avoid popliteal pressure.
      • • Use antiembolism and pneumatic compression stockings.
      • • Assess peripheral circulation.
      • • Administer prescribed prophylactic anticoagulant and antiplatelet drugs.
      • • Reinforce precautions to client and family.
      • • Encourage client to stop smoking and to exercise.
    • — Acute Management
      • • Oxygen therapy
      • • Monitor ABG and pulse oximetry.
      • • Check vital signs, lung sounds, and cardiac status.
    • • — Surgical Interventions
    • • Embolectomy
    • • Inferior vena cava filtration with placement of a vena cava filter
    • — High-Risk Medications
      • • Anticoagulants are used to prevent embolus enlargement and the formation of new clots; use with caution with active bleeding, stroke, and recent trauma.
      • • Warfarin (Coumadin) should be initiated within the first 3 days of heparinization and is continued for 3 to 6 months post PE.
      • • Low-molecular-weight heparin is usually used unless the PE is massive or occurs with hemodynamic instability.
      • • IV heparin for more severe cases
    • — Therapeutic aPTT values usually range from 1.5 to 2.5 times.
      • • Thrombolytics (fibrinolytic drugs)
        • • Alteplase (Activase, Cathflo), streptokinase
      • • Both anticoagulants and thrombolytic drugs are high-alert drugs and are administered by the RN.

Hypertension (HTN)

  • • Persistent BP elevation >140/90 mmHg
  • • Risk Factors
    • — Nonmodifiable: family history, gender, age, ethnicity
    • — Modifiable: use of alcohol, tobacco, caffeine; sedentary lifestyle; obesity
  • • Medications
    • — Diuretics, antihypertensives, angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, calcium channel blockers
  • • HTN Education (Fig. 5.7)
    • — The number one cause of stroke (CVA, brain attack) is nonadherence to HTN medications.

CHF, Congestive heart failure; MI, myocardial infarction; TIA, transient ischemic attack. A flowchart for how hypertension affects major target organs is as follows: • Heart: Arterioles constrict, leading to increased arterial pressure, which causes hypertrophy and weakened vessels. Weakened vessels are followed by either aneurysm or vessel rupture, which is followed by hemorrhage. Hypertrophy is followed by ventricular failure, leading to increased systemic venous pressure, which causes CHF, MI, and angina pectoris. • Kidneys: Atherosclerosis leads to narrow arterial lumen followed by necrosed renal arteries, atrophy, and finally results in renal failure. • Brain: Atherosclerosis is followed by either cerebral hemorrhage or decreased blood flow to cerebral arteries. Cerebral hemorrhage results in microaneurysms. Decreased blood flow to cerebral arteries is followed by cerebral infarctions resulting in TIA and stroke. • Eyes: Arteriosclerosis leads to ischemia, followed by retinal vein occlusion, which results in blindness and hemorrhage.

Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS): Angina

  • • Stable angina: predictable; subsides with rest.
  • • Unstable angina: unpredictable; may not subside with rest or nitroglycerin.
  • • Prinzmetal (variant) angina: vasospasm etiology; occurs at rest and may be precipitated by smoking or other substance.
  • • May radiate to either arm, shoulder, jaw, neck, or epigastric area.
  • • Other signs and symptoms: dyspnea, tachycardia, palpitations, nausea and vomiting, dyspepsia, fatigue, diaphoresis, pallor, syncope
  • • Often precipitated by exercise, cold exposure, heavy meal, stress, or intercourse.

Acute Coronary Syndrome: Acute Myocardial Infarction (MI)

  • • Destruction of myocardial tissue due to lack of blood and oxygen supply
  • • Begins with occlusion of the coronary artery
  • • Ischemia, injury, infarction
  • • ST-elevation MI (STEMI) (traditional MI) (Fig. 5.9)
  • • Non-STEMI (common in women)
  • • Ischemia
    • — Results from reduced blood flow and oxygen to the coronary arteries
    • — If not reversed, myocardial injury occurs
    • — Ischemia lasting 20 minutes or more is sufficient to produce irreversible tissue damage
    • — T-wave inversion
  • • Injury
    • — Prolonged interruption of oxygen supply and nutrients
    • — Cells are still salvageable
    • — ST elevation
  • • Infarction
    • —Tissue necrosis and death
    • —Irreversible damage
    • —Scar tissue has no electrical stimulation or contractility
    • —Within 24 hours of infarction, the healing process begins
    • —Pathological Q waves
  • • Complications
    • — As many as 90% of clients suffer complications, including the following:
      • • Dysrhythmias
      • • Cardiac failure
      • • Cardiogenic shock
      • • Thromboembolism
      • • Ventricular rupture
  • • Signs and Symptoms
    • — Pain
      • • Sudden onset; severity increases
      • • May persist for hours or days; not relieved by rest or nitroglycerin
      • • Heavy/constrictive
      • • Located behind the sternum
      • • May radiate to arms, back, neck, or jaw
    • — Cool and clammy skin
    • — Rapid, irregular, feeble pulse
  • • Atypical Symptoms
    • — Women
      • • Discomfort rather than pain
      • • Shortness of breath
      • • Extreme fatigue
    • — Clients with diabetes
      • • Asymptomatic
      • • Neuropathy
      • • Dyspnea
    • — Elderly clients
      • • Confusion/delirium
      • • Dizziness
      • • Shortness of breath
  • • Medical Diagnosis
    • — ECG (12-lead)
    • — Confirm by biomarkers (cardiac enzymes)
      • • Troponin
      • • Myoglobin

A chart shows three electrocardiographic tracings with characteristics from top to bottom as follows: Ischemia: The ECG tracing shows an arrow pointing at inverted T wave. Injury: The ECG tracing shows an arrow pointing at ST segment elevation reversible if reperfusion occurs. Infarction: The ECG tracing shows an arrow pointing at Q wave greater than one-third the height of the R wave, indicating necrosis through the entire wall (transmural).

Cardiac Lab Tests

  • • Troponin
    • — Found only in cardiac muscle
    • — May present as early as 1 hour after injury
    • — Peaks within 24 hours
    • — Returns to normal in 5 to 14 days
  • • Myoglobin
    • — Released 1 hour after an acute MI
    • — Rises before creatine kinase-MB levels
    • — Returns to normal within 24 hours

Treatment: Preserve Myocardial Tissue

  • • Overall goal is to preserve myocardial tissue within 90 minutes of arrival to the health care facility with the following:
    • —Drug therapy protocols (same as ACS)
      • • MONA
      • • THROMBINS2
    • —Beta-blockers
    • —Thrombolytic therapy via catheterization
    • —Telemetry; monitor every 4 to 6 hours per hospital protocol
    • —Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) and stents within 90 minutes of presentation for care
    • —If PCI unavailable, risk versus benefit of intravenous thrombolytic therapy is completed
    • —CABG
      • • Severe CAD detected
      • • Can be emergent or elective

Heart Failure

  • • Etiology
    • — CAD, prior MI
    • — Chronic HTN
    • — Cardiomyopathy
    • — Dilated, idiopathic
    • — Hyperthyroidism
    • — Diabetes
    • — Valvular and congenital heart disease
    • — Pulmonary diseases
  • • Left-Sided Heart Failure
    • —Causes: LV infarct, cardiomyopathy, HTN
    • —Symptoms: dyspnea, cough, orthopnea, pulmonary edema, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND)
    • —Signs: S3 gallop, tachycardia, inspiratory rales
    • —Laboratory findings: ABGs reveal hypoxemia, CXR shows pulmonary edema or pleural effusions, ↑B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) >500 ng/mL
  • • Right-Sided Heart Failure
    • — Causes: LHF, RV infarct, pulmonary or tricuspid valve disease, pulmonary HTN, COPD, PE
    • — Symptoms: systemic congestion, dyspnea on exertion (DOE), fatigue, weight gain, fluid retention
    • — Signs: ↑ central venous pressure (CVP), jugular venous distention (JVD) >3 to 4 cm, hepatomegaly, ascites, peripheral or sacral edema, and pleural and pericardial effusions
  • • Right Heart Failure Laboratory Tests
    • — Sodium and volume homeostasis
      • • As cardiac output decreases, renal perfusion decreases, activating the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), causing fluid retention
    • — Laboratory tests
      • • Liver function shows hepatic congestion
      • • Increased liver enzymes, increased PT and INR
      • • Hyponatremia (fluid restriction only if Na+ is <132 mEq/L) (<132 mmol/L)
      • • Increased BUN/creatinine = decreased renal perfusion
  • • Pharmacological Management
    • — ACE inhibitors
    • — Diuretics
    • — Inotropes
    • — Phosphodiesterase inhibitors
    • — Natriuretic peptides
    • — Beta-blockers
    • — Angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs)
    • — Vasodilators
    • — Dopamine agonist
    • — Anticoagulants/antiplatelets/aspirin
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Administer oxygen as prescribed
    • — Reposition and perform coughing, deep breathing exercises every 2 hours
    • — Limit sodium intake
    • — Fluid restriction only if Na+ <132 mEq/L (<132 mmol/L)
    • — Avoid excessive fluids
    • — Avoid alcohol: depresses myocardial contractility
    • — With CAD, low cholesterol, low fat, low Na+
    • — Educate on signs of worsening condition such as weight gain 3 lb (1.36 kg) weekly or 1–2 lb (0.45 to 0.9 kg) overnight; increasing DOE, orthopnea, or PND
    • — Regular exercise is strongly encouraged: Improves function of skeletal muscle more than changes in myocardial function

Dysrhythmias: Interpretation and Management

  • • Standard ECG uses 12 leads
    • — Provides best overall evaluation
  • • Telemetry
    • — Usually three leads show one view of the heart
  • • Holter monitor
    • — Usually worn for 24-hour continuous reading
  • • Client may be asymptomatic until cardiac output is altered.
  • • Client may complain of palpitations, syncope, pain, dyspnea, and diaphoresis.
  • • Changes in pulse rate/rhythm and ECG will be seen.
  • • Always treat the client and not the monitor!

Electrocardiogram (ECG) (Fig. 5.10)

  • • P Wave
    • — Atrial depolarization
  • • QRS Complex
    • — Ventricular depolarization
    • — Normal <0.11 second
  • • ST Segment
    • — Early ventricular repolarization
  • • T Wave
    • — Ventricular repolarization
  • • PR Interval
    • — Time for impulse to travel through SA (sinoatrial) node
    • — Normal: 0.12 to 0.2 second
  • • R-R Interval
    • — Measure regularity of heartbeat

Atrial Dysrhythmias

  • • Atrial Fibrillation (A-fib)
    • — Chaotic activity in the AV node
    • — No true P waves visible
    • — Irregular R-R intervals
    • — Risk for thrombus, CVA
    • — Anticoagulant therapy is necessary
  • • Atrial Flutter
    • — Saw-toothed waveform (F waves)
    • — Regular/irregular R-R interval
    • — Fluttering in chest
    • — Ventricular rhythm regular
  • • Cardioversion may be used for either.
  • • Focus is on rate control.
  • • If onset is >48 hours, then transesophageal echocardiogram (TEE) is required.
  • • Always ensure sedation prior to cardioversion.

Ventricular Dysrhythmias

  • • Ventricular Tachycardia (V-tach)
    • — Wide bizarre QRS complex
    • — Assess whether client has a pulse
    • — Risk for cardiac output impairment
    • — Prepare for synchronized cardioversion
      • • Defibrillate pulseless V-tach
    • — Administer antiarrhythmic drugs
  • • Ventricular Fibrillation (V-fib)
    • — Cardiac emergency
    • — No cardiac output
    • — Start cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR)
    • — Defibrillate as quickly as possible
    • — Administer antiarrhythmic drugs

Antiarrhythmic Medications

  • • Class I: Sodium channel blockers decrease conduction velocity in the atria, ventricles, and His-Purkinje system.
    • —IA: Disopyramide (Norpace), procainamide, quinidine
    • — IB: Lidocaine (Xylocaine), mexiletine, phenytoin (Dilantin)
    • —IC: Flecainide (Tambocor), propafenone (Rythmol)
    • —Other: Moricizine
  • • Class II: Beta-adrenergic blockers decrease automaticity of the SA node and decrease conduction velocity in AV node.
    • —Acebutolol, atenolol (Tenormin), metoprolol (Lopressor)
  • • Class III: Potassium channel blockers delay repolarization.
    • —Amiodarone, dofetilide (Tikosyn), sotalol (Betapace)
  • • Class IV: Calcium channel blockers decrease automaticity of SA node and delay AV node conduction.
    • —Diltiazem (Cardizem), verapamil (Calan)

Inflammatory Heart Disease

  • • Endocarditis
    • —Signs and symptoms: fever, positive blood cultures, murmur, hemorrhages, HF symptoms, often with IV drug abuse
    • —Infective endocarditis can lead to damaged heart valves.
    • —Assess for right- or left-sided heart failure.
    • —Administer IV antibiotics for 4 to 6 weeks.
    • —Maintain balance of rest and physical activity.
    • —Surgical treatment for valvular damage
    • —Reinforce client teaching to request prophylactic antibiotics for every invasive procedure.
  • • Pericarditis
    • —Signs and symptoms: pain—hurts more with deep breath or supine, pericardial friction rub.
    • —Monitor for ST-segment elevation.
    • — Monitor hemodynamic status.
    • — Facilitate a leaning-over position and nonsteroidal antiinflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) for pain control measures.

Valvular Heart Disease

  • • Valves may be unable to:
    • — Fully open (stenosis)
    • — Fully close (insufficiency or regurgitation)
  • • Causes
    • — Rheumatic fever
    • — Congenital heart disease
    • — Syphilis
    • — Endocarditis
    • — HTN
  • • Mitral Valve Stenosis
    • —Early period: may have no symptoms
    • —Later: excessive fatigue, DOE, orthopnea, dry cough — Progresses: hemoptysis or pulmonary edema
    • —Rumbling apical diastolic murmur and a-fib are common.
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — HF care
    • — Monitor for a-fib with thrombus formation.
    • — Prophylactic antibiotic therapy before any invasive procedures (dental, surgical, childbirth).
    • — May require surgical repair or valve replacement.

Vascular Disorders

  • • Arterial
    • — Smooth, shiny skin
    • — Pallor on elevation
    • — Weak peripheral pulses

    • — Cool to touch
    • — Intermittent claudication
    • — Painful, nonedematous ulcers

  • • Venous

    • — Bluish purple skin discoloration
    • — Normal peripheral pulses
    • — Warm to touch
    • — Slightly painful ulcers with marked edema

    • — Pruritus
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — General
      • • Encourage smoking cessation
      • • Encourage regular exercise

      • • Follow DASH diet

      • • Tight BP control
      • • Treatment of hyperlipidemia and hypertriglyceridemia

      • • Topical antibiotics
      • • Antiplatelet agent (clopidogrel)
      • • Surgical grafting intervention
    • — Venous

      • • Reinforce client teaching to elevate legs (at least 20 minutes, 4–5 times daily)

Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm

  • • Pulsating abdominal mass
  • • Bruit heard over abdomen
  • • Hypotension, JVD, widening pulse pressure
  • • Confirmed on x-ray (Fig. 5.11)
  • • If ruptures: signs and symptoms of hypovolemic shock
  • • Postoperative care:
    • — Monitor for signs and symptoms of renal failure, MI, ARDS, or postoperative ileus
    • — Monitor for change in pulse, signs and symptoms of occluded graft (changes in pulse, pain, cyanotic extremities)

Venous Thromboembolism
  • • Inflammation of the Venous Wall With Clot Formation
    • — Signs and symptoms: calf pain, induration (hardening) along the blood vessel, warmth, and redness
    • — Restrict ambulation
    • — Elevate extremity
    • — Antiembolic stockings
    • — Refrain from massaging leg muscles
  • • Medications
    • —Heparin therapy: monitor partial thromboplastin time (PTT); antidote is protamine sulfate
    • —Warfarin therapy: monitor prothrombin time (PT), international normalized ratio (INR); antidote is vitamin K
    • —Antiplatelet agents
      • • Clopidogrel bisulfate (Plavix)

Test-Taking Strategy

Candidates for the NCLEX should focus on obtaining knowledge of related nursing procedures for clients needing or undergoing diagnostic testing. A basic understanding of what diagnostic results can determine about a client’s condition can help a candidate anticipate physician orders.

Answer:

Select the physician orders the PN would anticipate from each of the following categories. (Note: Each category must have at least one option selected.)
Laboratory tests X CBC
X BMP
X Cardiac enzymes
X Lipid panel
Diagnostics X CXR
X 12-lead ECG
 ULS of abdomen
 EGD
Medications X Nitroglycerin spray
 Acetaminophen
X Magnesium hydroxide
 Lisinopril

Rationale: This client’s presenting symptoms require further assessment to rule out cardiac conditions. A CBC will rule out anemia, infection, and thrombocytopenia. BMP will evaluate electrolyte imbalances, which can lead to cardiac arrhythmias. Cardiac enzymes will assess for cardiac muscle damage. A lipid panel can indicate increased cholesterol, which can cause blockages in coronary arteries and lead to angina pectoris. A CXR will evaluate the chest cavity to rule out pneumonia and cardiac structure abnormalities. An ECG is used to diagnose cardiac rhythm abnormalities and MI. Sublingual nitroglycerin spray can treat angina pectoris and will increase oxygenation to the heart muscle. Giving an antacid can relieve indigestion, which could be another cause of the client’s discomfort. All other orders listed would not be initially ordered to rule out cardiac complications and would not be anticipated at this time.
