6

Ingestion, Digestion, Absorption, and Elimination

Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

  • • A backward flow of stomach acid up into the esophagus. (Fig. 6.1).
  • • Most common upper gastrointestinal (GI) problem seen in adults and can occur in all age groups.
  • • No one single cause of GERD.
  • • Predisposing Conditions
    • — Incompetent lower esophageal sphincter (LES)
    • — Hiatal hernia
    • — Decreased esophageal clearance (ability to clear liquids or food from the esophagus into the stomach) resulting from impaired esophageal motility
    • — Decreased gastric emptying
  • • Symptoms
    • — Heartburn (pyrosis) after eating
    • — Eructation
    • — Fullness and discomfort after eating
    • — Nocturnal coughing and wheezing
    • — Ask the client what foods seem to aggravate symptoms
    • — Positive diagnosis from barium swallow or fluoroscopy (hiatal hernia)
  • • Laboratory Testing
    • — Upper GI endoscopy
    • — Esophagram (barium swallow)
    • — pH monitoring
    • — Motility (manometry) testing
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Lifestyle modifications
      • • Encourage smoking cessation
      • • Weight management
      • • Encourage small, frequent meals
      • • Avoid fried, fatty foods; caffeine, carbonated beverages, citrus, tomatoes, excessive alcohol
      • • Sit up while eating and remain upright for 1 hour after eating
      • • Stop eating 3 hours before bedtime
      • • Elevate head of bed 4 to 6 inches
    • — Procedures to strengthen LES
    • — Monitor for Barrett esophagus
  • • GERD Medications
    • — Antacids (Fig. 6.2)
      • • Neutralize/reduce stomach acidity
    • — Histamine (H2) receptor antagonists
      • • Decrease acid secretions by blocking histamine receptors
    • — Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)
      • • Inhibit gastrin secretion
    • — Mucosal healing agents
    • — Promotility agents

Peptic Ulcer Disease

  • • Ulcerations of the mucous membrane or other layers of the GI tract (Fig. 6.3), most commonly occurring in the stomach and duodenum; most common between ages of 55 and 65 years.
  • • Significant gastric ulcers are caused by Helicobacter pylori bacteria.
  • • Risk Factors
    • — Drugs: Nonsteroidal antiinflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), corticosteroids, aspirin
    • — Alcohol, coffee
    • — Cigarette smoking
    • — Stress
  • • Symptoms
    • — Left epigastric pain, may radiate to back
      • • Nausea, weight loss, eructation, distention
    • — Symptoms occur 1 to 2 hours after meals
    • — Epigastric pain relieved with food; if penetrating, then food increases discomfort
    • — Diagnostic procedures:
      • • Barium swallow
      • • Upper endoscopy
      • • Helicobacter pylori testing
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Onset of symptoms
    • — What relieves symptoms
    • — Monitor stools for color, consistency, occult blood
    • — Drug therapy includes:
      • • H2 blockers
      • • PPIs
      • • Antacids
      • • Antibiotic therapy for H. pylori
    • — Surgery may be needed for complications.
      • • Perforation
      • • Hemorrhage
      • • Penetration
      • • Gastric outlet obstruction
  • • Complications
    • — Dumping syndrome—postoperative complication
      • • Symptoms include nausea, vomiting, diaphoresis, explosive diarrhea, and changes in blood glucose levels shortly after consuming a meal
      • • Lifestyle modification: eating six small meals daily that are high in protein and fat and low in carbohydrates; eating slowly, and avoiding fluids during meals; anticholinergics to decrease stomach motility
  • • Client Teaching
    • — Avoid medications such as:
      • • Salicylates
      • • NSAIDs
    • — Inform the health care provider (HCP) of history of peptic ulcer disease.
    • — Symptoms of GI bleeding:
      • • Dark, tarry stools
      • • Coffee-ground emesis
      • • Bright red rectal bleeding

Dumping Syndrome

  • • Complication After Gastric Surgery
    • — Rapid gastric emptying of hypertonic food from the stomach to the small intestine, drawing fluid into the bowel lumen, resulting in distention of the duodenum or jejunum; occurs in one-third to one-half of clients following surgery.
  • • Symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, diaphoresis, explosive diarrhea, and changes in blood glucose levels
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Six small meals high in protein and fat and low in carbohydrates
    • — Eat slowly; avoid fluids during meals
    • — Anticholinergic agents decrease stomach motility
    • — Recline for 1 hour after eating
    • — Symptoms disappear 1 year postop

Celiac Disease

  • • Causes
    • — Autoimmune disease characterized by damage to the small intestinal mucosa (Fig. 6.4) from ingesting wheat, barley, and rye
  • • Risk Factors
    • — Genetic predisposition, gluten ingestion, and immune-mediated response
  • • Symptoms
    • — Steatorrhea, abdominal distention, and malnutrition
  • • Possible Complications
    • — Decreased bone density, dental enamel hypoplasia, iron and folate deficiency, peripheral neuropathy, vesicular skin lesions (dermatitis herpetiformis)
  • • Laboratory Testing
    • — Serology testing and small intestine biopsy (early diagnosis and treatment is important to prevent complications)
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Gluten-free diet

Crohn’s Disease and Ulcerative Colitis

  • • Crohn’s Disease (Regional Enteritis)
    • — Causes
      • • Autoimmune, genetic, and environmental factors
    • — Location
      • • Affects small and large intestinal tract (most common site is terminal ileum and proximal cecum), all layers of the intestine
      • • Inflammation can be continuous or intermittent; extends through all layers of the intestine (Fig. 6.5)
      • • Affected areas have cobblestone appearance
    • — Symptoms
      • • Right lower quadrant abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, weight loss, fever, dehydration, steatorrhea (three to four stools per day), and unexplained anemia
      • • Onset occurs in early adolescence; second peak occurs in sixth decade
    • — Possible complications
      • • Bowel obstruction, fistulas, fissures, colonic dilation (toxic megacolon), and abscesses
    • — Diagnostic
      • • Barium enema with small bowel follow-through
      • • Capsule endoscopy
    • — Treatment
      • • Sedatives, tranquilizers, antidiarrheals, sulfasalazine, corticosteroids, immunosuppressives, antimicrobials
      • • Surgery for complications
    • — Nutrition
      • • Total parenteral nutrition, nothing by mouth (NPO) during severe inflammation
      • • Bland diet; no milk; vitamin and mineral supplementation
    • — Pharmacology
  • • Anti-inflammatory agents, multivitamins, and B12 injections
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • Goal is to achieve remission with first-line drugs
      • • Monitor weight
      • • Encourage at least 2 to 3 L of fluid/day
      • • Instruct client in low-residue, high-protein, high-calorie diet (limited dairy)
      • • Provide emotional support
  • • Ulcerative Colitis
    • — Location
      • • Large bowel and rectum
      • • DX with sigmoidoscopy
    • — Symptoms
      • • Diarrhea, abdominal pain, 10 to 20 loose stools/day, electrolyte imbalances, anemia
      • • Fever, tachycardia, weight loss, abdominal cramping, pain, anemia
      • • Complications: bleeding, rupture, and abdominal bloating; toxic megacolon
    • — Treatment
      • • Corticosteroids, antidiarrheals, sulfasalazine, mesalamine, infliximab, or other biological treatments
    • — Nutrition
      • • Low-residue, low-fat, high-protein diet; no dairy; calorie count; intake and output (I&O)

Diverticular Diseases

  • • Diverticulosis: Presence of pouch-like herniation of colon (Fig. 6.6)
  • • Diverticulitis: Inflammation of one or more of the diverticular sacs
  • • Inflammation: Can lead to perforation, abscess, hemorrhage, obstruction, peritonitis
  • • Risk factors: age >40, lack of exercise, obesity, smoking
  • • Symptoms
    • — Left lower quadrant pain, fever, elevated white blood cell (WBC) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
    • — Signs and symptoms of intestinal obstruction
    • — Abdominal distention
    • — Constipation/diarrhea
  • • Diagnostic
    • — Ultrasound, computed tomography (CT) scan, and barium enema/colonoscopy
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • • Sufficient fiber in diet (avoid foods containing seeds)
    • • High-fiber diet unless inflammation is present
    • • If inflammation present:
    • • NPO
    • • Then low-residue, bland diet
    • • Bulk-forming laxatives
    • • Avoid heavy lifting, tight clothing, and straining

Intestinal Obstruction

  • • Causes
    •   
      • • Mechanical
    • Adhesions most common
    • Strangulated hernia
    • Tumors
      • • Neurogenic
    • Paralytic ileus
    • Spinal cord lesion
      • • Vascular
    • Mesenteric artery occlusion
  • • Symptoms
    • — Sudden abdominal pain
    • — History of obstruction
    • — High-pitched bowel sounds with early mechanical obstruction
    • — Bowel sounds diminished or absent with neurogenic or late mechanical obstruction
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — NPO
    • — IV fluids
    • — Nasogastric (NG) tube to intermittent suction

Cirrhosis

  • • Chronic Progressive Disease
    • — Lobes of liver covered with scar tissue; liver degenerates and lobes are infiltrated with fat
    • — Forms of cirrhosis:
      • • Alcohol and/or drug related
      • • Postnecrotic
      • • Biliary
      • • Cardiac
      • • Autoimmune
      • • Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease
      • • Nonalcoholic steatohepatitis
    • — History
      • • Alcohol and/or drug abuse
      • • Exposure to hepatotoxins
      • • Chronic hepatitis C
  • • Symptoms
    • — Early signs: right upper quadrant (RUQ) pain, fatigue
    • — Jaundice
    • — Fruity or musty breath
    • — Asterixis
    • — Palmar erythema
    • — Ascites (Fig. 6.7)
    • — Dark-colored urine
    • — Clay-colored stools
    • — Pruritis
    • — Hepatic encephalopathy, mental confusion
    • — Weight loss
    • — Esophageal varices
    • — Coagulation disorders
  • • Diagnostic Testing
    • — Liver function tests: Elevated serum bilirubin, aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), lactic de-hydrogenase (LDH)
    • — Glucose tolerance test (GTT)
    • — Coagulation studies
    • — Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) to detect common bile duct obstruction
  • • Treatment
    • — Medications:
      • • Diuretics, beta blockers, ammonia reducers (lactulose), antibiotics, antivirals (for viral hepatitis)
    • — Vitamin K (for bleeding)
    • — Blood products, coagulation factors
    • — Liver transplant may be indicated
    • — Paracentesis or peritoneovenous shunt (LeVeen continuous peritoneal-jugular shunt) for treatment of ascites
  • • Dietary Teaching
    • — Alcohol restricted
    • — Low sodium
    • — Moderate-protein (limit protein in hepatic encephalopathy)
    • — High-carbohydrate diet with adequate vitamins
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Monitor for bleeding and instruct in bleeding precautions: Avoid injections, maintain pressure for 5 minutes after venipunctures, use electric razor
    • — Instruct in skin care: Avoid soap, perfumed lotions, and rubbing alcohol; use mild lotions; diphenhydramine may be used for pruritus
    • — Monitor fluids and electrolytes, accurate I&O, weigh daily, restrict fluids (<1500 mL/day), measure abdominal girth

Esophageal Varices

  • • Causes
    • — Complication of cirrhosis associated with portal hypertension
    • — Thrombosis in the portal vein
    • — Rupture is a medical emergency!
  • • Symptoms
    • — (Usually no symptoms unless veins bleed) emesis with heavy blood; black, tarry, bloody stools; lightheadedness; loss of consciousness, shock
  • • Diagnostic Testing: Endoscopic exam, CT, Doppler ultrasound, capsule endoscopy
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — If rupture occurs, this is a medical emergency
      • • Preventive propranolol is used to control portal hypertension and reduce the likelihood of rupture
    • — Interventions to manage hemorrhage
      • • Hemodynamic stability: FFP and packed red blood cells, vitamin K, H2 receptor blockers such as cimetidine, and electrolyte replacements
      • • Vasopressin administered intravenously or directly into the superior vena cava is used to decrease or stop the hemorrhaging (sometimes used concurrently with nitroglycerin)
      • • Esophagogastric balloon tamponade
      • • Octreotide is sometimes used in combination with band ligation

Hepatitis

  • • Widespread inflammation of liver cells, usually caused by a virus.
  • • See Appendix B: Comparison of Three Types of Hepatitis (Hepatitis A, B, and C).
  • • Viral Hepatitis
    • — Hepatitis A
      • • Most common, transmitted through fecal contamination; vaccine available
    • — Hepatitis B
      • • Long-term incubation, transmitted by contaminated blood, perinatal, sexual contact; vaccine available
    • — Hepatitis C
      • • Contaminated blood, sexual contact
    • — Hepatitis D
      • • Hepatitis with a coinfection with hepatitis B
    • — Hepatitis E
      • • Transmitted through fecal contamination, rare in United States
    • — Hepatitis G
      • • Found in blood donors and can be transmitted by transfusion
  • • Hepatitis viruses are the most common cause of hepatitis in the world, but other infections, toxic substances (e.g., alcohol, certain drugs), and autoimmune diseases may also cause hepatitis.
  • • Risk groups
    • — Persons with multiple sex partners
    • — IV drug users (disease transmitted by contaminated needles)
    • — Tattoo/body piercing with contaminated needles
    • — Living in crowded conditions
    • — Health care workers employed in high-risk areas
  • • Symptoms
    • — Fatigue, weakness, jaundice
    • — Anorexia, dark urine
    • — Joint pain, muscle aches
  • • Laboratory Testing
    • — ↑ Direct bilirubin, gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT), AST, ALT, LDH, and alkaline phosphatase levels; leukopenia; hypoglycemia; antigens
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Frequent rest periods
    • — Provide high-calorie, high-carbohydrate diet with moderate fats and proteins
    • — Administer antiemetic as needed
    • — Avoid alcohol intake and drugs detoxified by the liver
    • — Provide teaching to decrease transmission of infection
    • — For healthcare workers, frequent handwashing, needle precautions, immunizations

Pancreatitis

  • • Acute Pancreatitis: Autodigestion of the pancreas; alcohol ingestion and biliary tract disease are major causes.
    • — Symptoms
      • • Nausea and vomiting
      • • Low-grade fever
      • • Leukocytosis
      • • Jaundice
      • • Abdominal tenderness with muscle guarding
      • • Areas of ecchymoses, Grey Turner spots or sign, a bluish flank discoloration, and Cullen sign, a bluish periumbilical discoloration
      • • Elevated serum lipase and amylase levels
      • • Hypotension
      • • Tachycardia
      • • Hypovolemia (massive fluid shift into the retroperitoneal space)
      • • Shock (hemorrhage into the pancreas)
      • • Toxemia (activated pancreatic enzymes)
      • • The lungs are frequently involved (crackles)
      • • Bowel sounds may be decreased or absent, and ileus may occur
  • • Chronic pancreatitis: Progressive, destructive disease; long-term alcohol use is major factor in disease
    • — Symptoms
      • • Steatorrhea
      • • Diarrhea
      • • Jaundice
      • • Ascites
      • • Weight loss
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Acute Management
      • • NPO, NG tube to suction
      • • Antispasmodic agents
      • • Morphine or hydromorphone for pain management
      • • Sitting up or leaning forward may reduce pain
      • • Monitor blood sugar
      • • Teach foods and fluids to avoid
    • — Chronic Management
      • • Pain management
      • • Morphine
      • • Pancreatic enzymes
      • • Mix powdered forms with fruit juice or applesauce; avoid mixing with proteins
      • • H2 blocker agents
      • • Teach foods and fluids to avoid
      • • Support efforts to abstain from alcohol

Cholecystitis and Cholelithiasis

    • — Jaundice
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Analgesics for pain

    • — Low-fat diet
    • — Cholelithiasis
      • • Nonsurgical removal
      • • Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP)
      • • Lithotripsy
      • • Surgical approach
      • • Cholecystectomy, laparoscopic, or open

Urinary Tract Infections (Cystitis)

  • • Causes: Escherichia coli, sexual intercourse, urethritis
  • • Symptoms: Urinary urgency, burning sensation when urinating, cloudy urine, hematuria, pelvic pain, urine with strong odor
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Obtain clean-catch midstream specimen for C&S
    • — Administer antibiotics as ordered
      • • Take fully prescribed dose
      • • Do not skip doses
    • — Encourage fluid intake of 3000 mL/day
    • — Encourage voiding every 2 to 3 hours
    • — Avoid tight clothing and bubble baths
    • — Instruct females to wipe front to back to avoid urethral contamination with feces

Urinary Tract Obstruction

  • • Caused by calculi (stones)
  • • Location of pain can help locate stone
    • — Flank pain (stone usually in upper ureter)
    • — Pain radiating to the abdomen (stone likely in ureter or bladder)
  • • Laboratory Testing: Clean-catch urine specimen, ultrasound, voiding cystourethrogram, renal nuclear scan, cystoscopy
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Administer narcotics
    • — Strain all urine
    • — Encourage high fluid intake
      • • 3 to 4 L/day
    • — Strict I&O
    • — May need surgical management

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)

Enlargement of the Prostate (Fig. 6.9)

  • • Complications of BPH: Urinary tract infection, prostatitis, urethral stricture, kidney or bladder stones, nerve damage, cancer of the prostate or bladder, scarring of the bladder neck
  • • Laboratory Testing: Digital rectal exam, urinalysis, prostate-specific antigen test
  • • Symptoms
    • — Increased urinary frequency, nocturia
    • — Decreased urinary output
    • — Bladder distention (increases risk of spasm)
  • • Nonsurgical Options for Treatment
    • — Alpha blockers (terazosin, doxazosin, tamsulosin)
    • — 5-alpha reductase inhibitors—finasteride and dutasteride
  • • Surgical Options
    • — Transurethral microwave thermotherapy
    • — Transurethral needle ablation
    • — Photoselective vaporization of the prostate
    • — Most common treatment: transurethral resection of the prostate
      • • If prostate is too large, will use suprapubic approach
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Assess for:
      • • Bladder distention (increases risk of spasm)
    • — Preoperative teaching
      • • Pain management
      • • Oversized balloon catheter
    • — Bladder spasms
      • • Common after surgery
      • • Use antispasmodics
      • • Belladonna and opium suppositories
      • • Oxybutynin chloride
      • • Dicyclomine hydrochloride
    • — Continuous bladder irrigation
      • • Drainage should be reddish pink for 24 hours, clearing to light pink
      • • Monitor the color and amount of urine output
      • • Notify the HCP if there is ↑ bleeding and clots
  • • Discharge Teaching
    • — Continue to drink 12 to 14 glasses of water per day
    • — Avoid straining
    • — Avoid strenuous activity, sports, lifting, and intercourse for 3 to 4 weeks
    • — Report large amounts of blood or frank blood

Acute Kidney Injury

  • • A reversible syndrome if symptoms are caught early.
  • • Remember:
    • — Kidneys use 25% of normal cardiac output to maintain function
    • — Kidneys excrete 1 to 2 L of urine per 24 hours for adults
  • • Prerenal Failure
    • — Hemorrhage/hypovolemia
    • — ↓ Cardiac output
    • — ↓ Renal perfusion
  • • Intrarenal Failure
    • — May develop secondary to prerenal failure
    • — Nephrotoxins, infections
    • — Renal injury, obstruction
    • — Vascular lesions
  • • Postrenal Failure
    • — Obstruction, calculi, BPH
    • — Tumors, strictures
  • • Nursing Assessment
    • — ↓ Urine output
    • — Weight gain
    • — Edema
    • — Diagnostic test results: Oliguric phase
      • • ↑ Blood urea nitrogen (BUN) and creatinine
      • • ↑ Potassium
      • • ↓ Sodium (serum)
      • • ↓ pH (Metabolic acidosis)
      • • ↑ Urine sodium
      • • ↑ Specific gravity
    • — Diagnostic test results: Diuretic phase
      • • ↓ Fluid volume
      • • ↓ Potassium
      • • ↓ Sodium
      • • ↓ Urine specific gravity
      • • ↓ Urine sodium
    • — Recovery phase: Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) slowly increases; full recovery may take a year
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Oliguric phase: Give only enough fluids to replace losses, + 400 to 500 mL/24 hours
    • — Strict I&O
    • — Monitor lab values closely
    • — Watch for electrocardiogram changes
    • — Monitor weight daily

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

  • • End-Stage Renal Disease
  • • Progressive irreversible damage to the nephrons and glomeruli
  • • Causes: Diabetic nephropathy, hypertensive nephrosclerosis, glomerulonephritis, polycystic kidney disease
  • • Symptoms
    • — Early stage
      • • Polyuria
      • • Renal insufficiency
    • — Late stage
      • • Oliguria
      • • Hematuria
      • • Proteinuria
      • • Edema
      • • ↑ Blood pressure (BP)
      • • Muscle wasting, secondary to negative nitrogen balance
      • • Ammonia taste in mouth
      • • ↑ Creatinine, ↑ PO4, ↑ K
    • — End stage
      • • Anuria (<100 mL/24 hours)
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Monitor serum electrolytes
    • — Weigh daily
    • — Strict I&O
    • — Renal diet
      • • Low protein
      • • Low sodium
      • • Low potassium
      • • Low phosphate
  • • Medications
    • — Drugs are used to manage the associated complications
      • • Aluminum hydroxide to bind phosphates
      • • Epoetin alfa to treat anemia
      • • Antihypertensive therapy
      • • Calcium supplements and vitamin D
      • • Antihyperlipidemics
      • • Statins to lower low-density lipoprotein
      • • Fibrates to lower triglycerides
    • — CAUTION: As kidney function decreases, medication doses require adjustment
  • • Renal Dialysis
    • — Hemodialysis (Fig. 6.10)
      • • AV fistula
      • • Ø Venipunctures, Ø IVs, Ø BP in arteriovenous (AV) shunt arm
      • • Withhold medications that would affect hemodynamic stability before dialysis
  • • Peritoneal Dialysis (Fig. 6.11)
    • — Monitor indwell and outflow times closely
    • — Monitor I&O, signs of pulmonary edema
      • • Daily weight
      • • Monitor outflow for color, clarity
      • • — Contraindications include peritonitis, recent abdominal surgery, GI problems
  • • Postoperative Care: Kidney Surgery
    • — Respiratory status
      • • Auscultate to detect crackles
      • • Demonstrate splinting method to encourage coughing
      • • — Circulatory status
      • • Monitor for signs and symptoms of shock
      • • Monitor the surgical site for bleeding
    • — Administer narcotic analgesics as needed

Test-Taking Strategy

A helpful method for decreasing test anxiety is to practice self-affirmation. After you have adequately studied and really understand the material, look in the mirror and say to yourself out loud, “I know this material, and I will do well on the test.” After several times of watching and hearing yourself reaffirm your knowledge, you will gain inner confidence and be able to perform much better during the NCLEX.

Next Generation NCLEX® Examination (NGN)–Style Question

Multiple Response: Select N

Scenario: The RN is admitting a client to the medical unit. The client is a 77-year-old with a 3-day history of nonlocalized abdominal discomfort. Assessment findings: Most current vital signs: BP: 116/64; P: 85; R: 21; temperature: 99.4 °F; pain: 4/10. A&O to person only. Incontinence, new-onset mental confusion, and loose stools. The postvoid catheterization yielded 150 mL of dark, cloudy urine that had a strong odor. Lungs are clear to auscultation, no cough present. No peripheral edema. Pulses palpable. Skin is pink, dry.

Select four assessment findings that require immediate follow-up.
 

1. Vital signs

 

2. Lung sounds

 

3. Mental status

 

4. Skin temperature and moisture

 

5. Urine character

 

6. Loose stools

 

7. Pain

Answer: 3, 5, 6, 7

Rationale: The client’s new onset of mental confusion is of high concern and needs immediate evaluation. This along with the urine character and pain could be indicative of a UTI, which could be very concerning in a client of this age. Loose stools can be caused by a UTI, but it can also cause dehydration, which would exacerbate the infection and is also very concerning for a patient this age. These findings indicate need for further follow-up. The vital signs are WNL at this time, lungs are clear, and skin is dry. These are normal findings and can be monitored routinely.
