Clinical Concepts and Mechanisms of Disease

Operative Care: Preoperative Care

  • • Preparation
    • — Obtain a complete history, including the following:
  • • Physical assessment
  • • List of current medications (prescriptions, over the counter [OTC], complementary, and supplements)
  • • List of allergies and reactions
  • • Previous surgical experiences (response to anesthetic)
  • • Signed consent (confirm informed consent before client is sedated)
  • • Confirm labs and diagnostics
  • • Skin preparation
  • • Preop checklist and preprocedure verification
  • • Preoperative Teaching
    • — Diet restrictions (typically NPO [nothing by mouth] after midnight before surgery)
    • — Discuss medications that should/or should not be taken the day of surgery
    • — Teach coughing and deep breathing, incentive spirometry
    • — Review methods of pain control
    • — Use of thromboembolic deterrent stockings, sequential compression devices (SCDs), and expected outcomes

Postoperative Care (Fig. 3.1)

  • • ABCs and hemodynamic stability (monitor for signs and symptoms of shock)
  • • Position client supine or low fowler’s, if not contraindicated
  • • Manage pain
  • • Maintain core body temperature

Nursing and Collaborative Care

  • • Urinary retention
    • — Check for bladder distention
  • • Urinary tract infection (UTI) Prevention
    • — Remove urinary catheter Postoperative Day (POD) #1 or #2, unless contraindicated
  • • Pulmonary problems
    • — Check Breath Sounds
    • — Check O2 saturation
  • • Bowel sounds
    • — Decreased Peristalsis
    • — Paralytic Ileus
  • • Wound management
    • — Incisions, Drains, Skin Integrity
    • — Wound dehiscence or evisceration
  • • Venous thromboembolism (VTE) prophylaxis
    • — Thromboembolic deterrent (TED) Hose or sequential compression devices (SCDs)
    • — Pharmacological agents for VTE prophylaxis
    • — Early mobilization (Fig. 3.2)

Pain

  • • Pain management is client centered.
  • • Pain is whatever the client says it is, existing whenever he or she reports pain.
  • • Pain occurs in all clinical settings.
  • • Nurses have both a legal and ethical responsibility for managing clients’ pain and suffering (be aware of any personal bias or misconceptions about pain management).
  • • Pain Assessment and Management
    • — Assessment includes:
      • • P: Precipitating or palliative
      • • Q: Quality
      • • R: Relief measures/region (location)
      • • S: Severity (using a scale appropriate for age and client condition: numerical, descriptive, a set of faces with expressions)
      • • T: Timing (onset, duration)
      • • U: Effect of pain on client
    • — Assess for nonverbal behaviors, which may indicate pain (moaning, grimacing, clenched teeth, pacing, or inactivity)
    • — Wong-Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale recommended for persons 3 years of age and older (Fig. 3.3, B)
    • — Assess personal cultural, spiritual, and ethical beliefs that may influence the perception of pain
    • — Consider special populations: palliative care, end-of-life
    • — Documentation includes pain assessment data (preintervention and postintervention rating), interventions (both nonpharmacological and pharmacological), and client teaching
    • — Controlled substance disposal must be documented per regulatory guidelines (ask another nurse to witness the waste of a tablet fragment, discarded injection contents, or discarded liquid contents)

  • • Nonpharmacological Noninvasive Pain Relief Techniques
    • — Repositioning
    • — Cutaneous stimulation: Heat and cold application, massage therapy, transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS)
    • — Relaxation techniques
    • — Guided imagery
    • — Complementary and alternative medicine (CAM) (e.g., acupressure or yoga)
    • — Environmental control (e.g., quiet atmosphere, warm environment)
  • • Pharmacological Types of Pain Medications
    • — Nonopioids for mild pain or use in combinations for moderate pain
    • — Opioids for moderate to severe pain
    • — Coanalgesic or adjuvant drugs (i.e., anticonvulsants, antidepressants) for neuropathic pain

Fluids and Electrolytes

  • • Fluid Balance
    • — Changes in osmolarity cause shifts in fluid; the osmolarity of the extracellular fluid (ECF) is almost entirely due to sodium
    • — The osmolarity of intracellular fluid (ICF) is related to many particles, with potassium being the primary electrolyte
    • — The pressures in the ECF and the ICF are almost identical; if either ECF or ICF changes in concentration, fluid shifts from the area of lesser concentration to the area of greater concentration
  • • Fluid Volume Excess
    • — Causes
      • • Congestive heart failure (CHF) (most common), renal failure, cirrhosis, overhydration
    • — Symptoms
      • • Peripheral edema (Fig. 3.4), periorbital edema, elevated blood pressure (BP), dyspnea, altered level of consciousness (LOC)
    • — Lab findings
      • • ↓ Blood urea nitrogen (BUN), ↓ Hemoglobin (Hbg), ↓ Hematocrit (Hct), ↓ serum osmolality, ↓ urine specific gravity
    • — Treatment
      • • Diuretics, fluid restrictions, weigh daily, monitor K+

A closeup of left lower leg shows edema in complete area with pit created by finger present at the lateral side of the lower leg

  • • Fluid Volume Deficit
    • — Causes
      • • Inadequate fluid intake, hemorrhage, vomiting, diarrhea, burns, massive edema
    • — Symptoms
      • • Weight loss, oliguria, postural hypotension, dry mucous membranes
    • — Lab findings
      • • ↑ BUN and ↑ or normal creatinine, ↑ Hgb, ↑ Hct, ↑ urine specific gravity
    • — Treatment
      • • Strict Intake and Output (I&O), replace with isotonic fluids, monitor BP, weigh daily
  • • Types of IV Fluids
    • — Isotonic fluids expand the ECF volume (osmolarity between 250 and 300 mOsm/L): 0.9% normal saline (NS), lactated Ringer (LR) solution
    • — Hypotonic fluids move water into the cell by osmosis, causing them to swell (osmolarity less than 250 mOsm/L): 0.45% NS
    • — Hypertonic fluids pull fluid from cells, causing them to shrink (osmolarity >300 mOsm/L): D10; 3% saline, D5 in 0.45% NS

Electrolyte Imbalances

Sodium: Skeletal muscle contraction, cardiac contraction, nerve impulse transmission, normal osmolarity, and volume of ECF

  • • Hyponatremia
    • — Causes: Diuretics, gastrointestinal (GI) fluid loss, hypotonic IV fluids, diaphoresis
    • — Diagnostic: Na+ <136 mEq/L (<136 mmol/L)
    • — Signs & symptoms: Muscle cramps, confusion, weakness, seizures
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • • Check BP frequently
    • • Restrict fluids
    • • Diet (foods high in Na+ content)
      • • Cheese
      • • Table salt
      • • Seafood
      • • Processed meat
      • • Canned foods
      • • Ketchup
      • • Salty snack foods
  • • Hypernatremia
    • —  Causes: Water deprivation, diabetes insipidus, renal failure, Cushing syndrome
    • — Signs & symptoms: Dry mucous membranes, thick mucus, low urinary output, decreased skin turgor, restlessness, agitation, confusion, flushed skin, pulmonary edema, seizures, thirst, fever
    • — Diagnostic: Na+ >145 mEq/L (>145 mmol/L)
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • No IVs that contain sodium
      • • Restrict dietary sodium
      • • Weigh daily

Potassium: Depolarizes and generates action potentials, regulating protein synthesis and glucose use and storage

  • • Hypokalemia
    • —   Causes: Diuretics, vomiting, diarrhea, Cushing syndrome, gastric suction
    • — Diagnostic: K+ <3.5 mEq/L (mmol/L)
    • — Signs & Symptoms: Rapid, thready pulse, flat T waves, anorexia, muscle cramps, weakness, fatigue, tingling, numbness, nausea, vomiting, constipation, GI bloating, palpitations
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • IV potassium
      • • Diet: foods high in K+
      • • Oranges
      • • Bananas
      • • Apricots
      • • Cantaloupe
      • • Legumes
      • • Leafy vegetables
      • • Potatoes
      • • Meat
      • • Salt substitute
  • • Hyperkalemia
    • — Causes: Oliguria, acidosis, renal failure, Addison disease
    • — Diagnostic: K+ >5 mEq/L (mmol/L)
    • — Signs & Symptoms: Tall tented T waves, bradycardia, muscle weakness, tiredness, numbness, tingling, nausea, vomiting, respiratory distress, angina, palpitations, irregular heartbeat
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • 10 U of regular insulin and 50 mL of dextrose 50% in water (D50W)
      • • Sodium polystyrene sulfonate is used to treat hyperkalemia
      • • IV loop diuretics
      • • Renal dialysis

Calcium: Maintains bone strength and density, activates enzymes, allows skeletal and cardiac muscle contraction, controls nerve impulse transmission, and allows for blood clotting

  • • Hypocalcemia
    • — Causes: Renal failure, hypoparathyroidism malabsorption, pancreatitis, alkalosis
    • — Diagnostic: Ca++ <9 mg/dL (<2.25 mmol/L)
    • — Signs & Symptoms: Positive Trousseau sign (Fig. 3.5) (carpopedal spasm which occurs when a BP cuff is inflated above systolic BP [SBP] for 3 minutes); diarrhea; numbness; convulsions; numbness and tingling of fingers, toes, and circumoral (around mouth) region; positive Chvostek’s sign (Fig. 3.6) (contraction of facial muscles when facial nerve is tapped); hyperactive reflexes; muscle twitching and cramping; carpal and pedal spasms; tetany; seizures; laryngospasm; dysrhythmias
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • Administer calcium supplements
      • • Administer IV calcium slowly
      • • Encourage calcium-rich foods, vitamin D, protein
      • • For acute hypocalcemia, keep a tracheostomy tray and resuscitation bag at bedside in case of laryngeal spasms
  • • Hypercalcemia
    • — Causes: Hyperparathyroidism, malignant bone disease, excessive supplementation
    • — Diagnostic: Ca++ >10.5 mg/dL (>2.62 mmol/L)
    • — Signs & Symptoms: Muscle weakness, constipation, nausea and vomiting, dysrhythmias, behavioral changes, anorexia, fatigue, diminished reflexes, lethargy, decreased level of consciousness, confusion, personality change, cardiac arrest if severe
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • Limit vitamin D intake
      • • Avoid calcium-based antacids
      • • Administer calcitonin to reduce calcium
      • • Renal dialysis may be required

Magnesium: Controls skeletal muscle contraction, carbohydrate metabolism, adenosine triphosphate (ATP) formation, vitamin activation, and cell growth

  • • Hypomagnesemia
    • — Causes: Alcoholism, malabsorption, diabetic ketoacidosis, diuretics
    • — Diagnostic: Mg2+ <1.3 mEq/L (<0.65 mmol/L)
    • — Signs & Symptoms: Skeletal muscle weakness; hyperactive deep tendon reflexes; numbness and tingling; painful muscle contractions; decreased GI motility; nausea
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • Administer oral supplements
      • • Increase dietary intake of magnesium
      • • IV magnesium via infusion pump
      • • Monitor vital signs
  • • Hypermagnesemia
    • — Causes: Renal failure, adrenal insufficiency, excess replacement
    • — Diagnostic: Mg2+ >2.1 mEq/L (>1.05 mmol/L)
    • — Signs & Symptoms: Bradycardia; peripheral vasodilation; hypotension; prolonged PR interval with a widened QRS complex; decreased to absent deep tendon reflexes
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • No magnesium drugs for chronic kidney disease (CKD) clients
      • • IV administration of calcium chloride or calcium gluconate

Phosphorous: Activates vitamins and enzymes, forms ATP for energy supplies, assists in cell growth and metabolism, maintains acid-base balance and calcium homeostasis

  • • Hypophosphatemia
    • — Causes: Alcohol withdrawal, diabetic ketoacidosis, respiratory alkalosis
    • —Diagnostic: Phosphate <3.0 mg/dL (<0.97 mmol/L)
    • — Signs & Symptoms: ↓ Cardiac output, weak peripheral pulses, skeletal muscle weakness
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • Oral supplementation
      • • Diet high in phosphorus
  • • Hyperphosphatemia
    • — Causes: Renal failure, excess intake
    • —Diagnostic: Phosphate >4.5 mg/dL (>1.45 mmol/L)
    • — Signs & Symptoms: Includes signs of hypocalcemia
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management
      • • Phosphate binders
      • • Diet low in phosphorus

Acid Base

  • • Acid-Base Balance
    • — pH
      • • Normal = 7.35 to 7.45
      • • <7.35 = acidosis
      • • >7.45 = alkalosis
    • — pCO2
      • • Normal = 35 to 45 mm Hg
      • • >45 = acidosis
      • • <35 = alkalosis
    • — HCO3
      • • Normal = 21–28 mEq/L (21–28 mmol/L)
      • • <21 = acidosis
      • • >28 = alkalosis
    • — PaO2
      • • Normal = 80 to 100 mm Hg
    • — Anion gap
      • • 10 to 16 mEq/L (10–16 mmol/L)

Arterial Blood Gas Interpretation Practice

  1. 1. Determine whether the pH value is normal, acidic, or alkalotic.
    1. 1. 7.31 ______
    2. 2. 7.47 ______
    3. 3. 7.36 ______

Rationale

  •   
    1. 1. 7.31 Acidic
    2. 2. 7.47 Alkalotic
    3. 3. 7.36 Normal
  • 2. Determine whether the client is hypoventilating, hyperventilating, or has normal ventilation.
    1. 1. pCO2 = 42
    2. 2. pCO2 = 33
    3. 3. pCO2 = 55

Rationale

  1. 1. pCO2 = 42, Normal ventilation
  2. 2. pCO2 = 33, Hyperventilating: blowing off CO2
  3. 3. pCO2 = 55, Hypoventilating: retaining CO2
  1. 3. Determine whether the value indicates a state of acidosis, alkalosis, or normal.
    1. 1. HCO3 = 20
    2. 2. HCO3 = 33
    3. 3. HCO3 = 21

Rationale

  1. 1. HCO3 = 20 Acidosis
  2. 2. HCO3 = 33 Alkalosis
  3. 3. HCO3 = 21 Normal

Steps for Interpreting Arterial Blood Gas (ABG) Results

  1. 1. Is there alkalosis or acidosis present? First look at pH results.
    pH <7.35 = acidosis
    pH >7.45 = alkalosis
  2. 2. What is the respiratory (pCO2) component?
    • • The number is the amount of CO2 in the blood
    • • CO2 is the respiratory component in the blood measured by millimeters of Mercury (mm Hg)
    • • Normal CO2 level is between 35–45 mm Hg
    • • Respiratory alkalosis is pCO2 below 35 mm Hg; symptom is hyperventilation
    • • Respiratory acidosis is pCO2 above 45 mm Hg; related to hypoventilation, symptom is confusion or restlessness
  3. 3. Check your blood gas report for the bicarbonate (HCO3) level. It is the renal component in the acid-base balance. The normal level for these ions is between 21 and 28 mEq/L.
  4. 4. Determine whether there is compensation for the pH imbalance. Generally, the pulmonary and renal systems compensate for each other to return the pH to normal. The lungs will compensate for metabolic instability by changing CO2 excretion and the kidneys will compensate for the respiratory instability by altering bicarbonate retention and H+ excretion.

Safety: Sentinel Events

  • • An unexpected outcome involving a death or serious injury.
  • • Signals the need for immediate investigation and response.
  • • Accredited hospitals are expected to identify and respond to all sentinel events.
  • • Examples of Sentinel Events
    • — Client suicide
    • — Operative/postoperative complication
    • — Wrong-site surgery
    • — Medication error
    • — Client fall
    • — Client death or injury in restraints
    • — Transfusion error
  • • Response to Sentinel Events
    • — Analyzing root cause
    • — Designing action plan to implement improvements to ↓ risk
    • — Implementing improvements
    • — Monitoring effectiveness of those improvements

High-Alert Medications

  • • Most likely to cause significant harm to a client even when used as intended.
    • — Types of Drugs
      • • Anticoagulants, narcotics and opiates, insulin, chemotherapeutic drugs, and sedatives
    • — Types of Harm
      • • Hypotension, bleeding, hypoglycemia, delirium, lethargy, bradycardia, respiratory depression or distress
    • — Prevention Strategies
      • • Built-in redundancies
      • • Double-checking
      • • Smart pumps
      • • Standardized or protocol-driven order sets
      • • Two-person verification process

Death and Grief

  • • Stages of Grief
    • — Denial
    • — Anger
    • — Bargaining
    • — Depression
    • — Acceptance
      • • Facilitate grieving process by using therapeutic touch, displaying warm and caring behaviors, and using open-ended statements to listen to and assist those who are grieving to understand their own feelings and behaviors.
      • • Encourage client to express anger.
      • • Do not take away the defense mechanisms for coping that the client uses in crisis.
      • • Customs surrounding death and dying vary among cultures.
      • • Make every attempt to understand and accommodate the family’s cultural traditions when caring for the dying client.

Infection

  • • Infection is the invasion of the body by a pathogen
  • • Response to the invasion can be:
    • — Localized
    • — Systemic
  • • Nosocomial, or hospital-acquired, infections
    • — Acquired as a result of exposure to a microorganism in a hospital setting

Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)

  • • Routes of Transmission
    • — Unprotected sexual contact
      • • Most common mode of transmission
    • — Exposure to blood through drug-using equipment
    • — Perinatal transmission
      • • During pregnancy
      • • At the time of delivery
      • • Breastfeeding
  • • Laboratory Testing for HIV
    • — Antibody tests
      • • Look for antibodies to HIV in a person’s blood or oral fluid (e.g., enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay [ELISA])
    • — Antigen/antibody tests
      • • Look for both HIV antibodies and antigens
    • — NATs (nucleic acid tests)
      • • Look for the actual virus in the blood
    • — In-Home HIV Test: Positive result is only preliminary; it must be confirmed by an HCP
    • — Diagnosis of AIDS (HIV-III)
      • • HIV-positive and a CD4+ T-cell count <200 cells/mm3 or an opportunistic infection
  • • HIV Symptoms
    • — Primary HIV infection
      • • Earliest stage: begins with flulike symptoms and antibodies may develop in weeks 1 to 12
    • — Early (symptomatic) HIV disease signs and symptoms may not be present until 10 to 14 years after initial exposure without treatment
      • • Fatigue, severe weight loss, swollen glands, unexplained fever, night sweats, dry cough
    • —  Parasites, fungi, bacteria, and viruses: cause of opportunistic diseases
      • • Candidiasis: lungs, bronchi, esophagus
      • • Invasive cervical cancer
      • • Cytomegalovirus (CMV)
      • • — HIV-related encephalopathy
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Monitor disease progression and immune function
    • — Prevent development of opportunistic diseases
    • — Detect and treat opportunistic diseases
    • — Manage symptoms
    • — Prevent or decrease complications of treatment
    • — Prevent transmission of HIV
    • — Encourage proper nutrition
    • — Refer to mental health counseling
    • — Encourage safe sex practices
  • • HIV Drug Therapy
    • —  Initiate and monitor combination antiretroviral therapy (cART), formerly referred to as highly active antiviral therapy (HAART)
    • —  Three Goals of Drug Therapy:
      • • Decrease the viral load
      • • Maintain or raise CD4+ T-cell counts
      • • Delay the development of HIV-related symptoms and opportunistic diseases
    • —  The client should have regular blood counts to track CD4 levels and the viral load
    • — Side effects are common, and there are many drug–drug interactions
    • — HIV Medications
      • • Nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs)
      • • Non–nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NNRTIs)
      • • Protease inhibitors (PIs)
      • • Integrase inhibitors
      • • Fusion inhibitors
      • • Entry inhibitor
      • • Postattachment inhibitors
  • • Pediatric HIV: Clinical Manifestations
    • — Recurrent infections such as thrush (oral candidiasis)
    • — Unexplained fever
    • — Lymphadenopathy
    • — Hepatosplenomegaly
    • — Failure to thrive
    • — Developmental delay
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Teach transmission and control of infectious diseases
    • — Safety issues include appropriate storage of special medications and equipment
    • — Prevention is a key component of HIV education
    • — Aggressive pain management is essential
    • — Common psychosocial concerns include disclosure of the diagnosis
    • — Do not administer live viruses
    • — Address psychosocial concerns
    • — If an HIV-infected mother is treated with zidovudine during pregnancy and the neonate is treated after birth, the probability of HIV infection of the child decreases

Cancer

  • • The leading sites of primary cancer in men are the prostate, lungs, colon, and rectum; the leading sites of primary cancer in women are the breasts, lungs, colon, and rectum.
  • • Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer-related deaths in both men and women.
  • • The incidence of cancer is highest among African Americans.
  • • Primary Prevention
    • — Education about lifestyle changes and risk factors
      • • Smoking/smokeless tobacco
      • • Dietary habits
      • • Ultraviolet radiation
      • • Excessive alcohol use
      • • Environmental/chemical carcinogens
    • — Cancer risk assessment

Test-Taking Strategy

When analyzing a question: 1) eliminate all the options you can; 2) choose the best answer to the extent of your knowledge and ability; 3) and then move on—emotionally and consciously. With the NCLEX, you cannot go back to any previous questions and change your answer, so forget about the question once you move past it. If you feel split between two options, pick the option that your gut instinct is telling you. Trust your gut instinct. You may not know exactly why, but something deep inside your consciousness is telling you the answer. Also, it is wiser to keep going through the exam than to sit and worry over a single question. Worrying and obsessing over a question you didn’t know will only distract your focus from the rest of the exam.

Body System  Interventions to Perform
Cardiovascular X Obtain a CBC.
X Assess distal pulses in all extremities.
X Elevate affected extremity.
Neurological  Assess sensation in left lower extremity.
 Initiate seizure precautions.
X Ask client to move toes on both feet.
Genitourinary  Strict I&O measurements.
 Administer diuretics for swelling.
X Obtain a pregnancy test.

Rationale:

The client is being prepared for surgery. It is important to obtain preoperative laboratory data such as a CBC for a baseline measurement and to make sure that the client can tolerate the surgery. It is very important when a client has a severe fracture like this to assess pulses, skin temperature, movement, and sensation in the extremity. A loss of pulses in the extremity could indicate compartment syndrome which is a medical emergency. Elevating the extremity can reduce swelling and decrease pain. Asking the patient to move her toes in both feet will help to assess neurological function in the extremities. A pregnancy test should be obtained for all females of child-bearing age before surgery to avoid any complications to the fetus. Assessing the sensation should be focused in the right extremity (the side of injury), not the left. There is no indication in the scenario that seizure precautions are warranted for this client. Strict I&Os are not needed at this time. The swelling this patient is experiencing in the extremity is related to inflammation due to injury; administering a diuretic will not resolve this issue and could create other unnecessary concerns.
