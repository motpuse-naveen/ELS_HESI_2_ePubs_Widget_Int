Maternal-Newborn Nursing

Pregnancy

  • • Assess for Intimate Partner Violence (IPV)
    • — Battering and emotional or physical abuse often begin with pregnancy (Fig. 10.1)
    • — Assess for abuse in private
    • — The nurse needs to know:
      • • Local community resources
      • • Where to recommend safety venues or places for the client
  • • Gravidity and Parity
    • — Gravida and parity: Counts pregnancies, not off-spring
    • — Gravida: Number of times a female has been pregnant, regardless of outcome
    • — Para: Number of deliveries (not children) occurred after 20 weeks’ gestation
    • — Multiple births count as one gravidity and one parity
    • — Pregnancy loss before 20 weeks counted as abortion but add 1 to gravidity
    • — Fetal demise after 20 weeks added to parity
  • • Past and future GTPAL represents obstetric history:
    • — G = Gravidity (total number of pregnancies)
    • — T = Term pregnancies (after 37 weeks)
    • — P = Preterm pregnancies (before 37 weeks)
    • — A = Abortions (elective or spontaneous): Loss before 20 weeks
    • — L = Living children
  • • Gestation
    • — Naegele rule
      • • — Count back 3 months from the date of last normal menstrual period
      • • — Add 1 year and 7 days
  • • Fundal Height
    • — 12 to 13 weeks: Fundus rises out of symphysis
    • — 20 weeks: Fundus at umbilicus
    • — 24 to about 36 weeks: Fundal height (in centimeters) from the symphysis is equal to number of weeks of gestation, but only if this is a single pregnancy
  • • Weight Gain (Optimal weight gain depends on maternal and fetal factors)
    • — First-trimester average total weight gain is 2–4 lbs (1–2 kg)
    • — Approximately 1 lb (0.5 kg) per week for a woman of normal weight during the second and third trimesters
  • • Maternal Psychological Changes
    • — Ambivalence
      • • Occurs early in pregnancy, even with a planned pregnancy
    • — Acceptance
      • • Occurs with the woman’s readiness for the experience; prolonged nonacceptance is a warning sign not directly related to nonacceptance of the child
  • • — Emotional lability
    •   
      • • Rapid, unpredictable changes in mood
  • • Warning Signs of Physical Maternal Changes
    • — Abdominal pain/cramping or pelvic pain
    • — Decreased or absent fetal movement
    • — Fever or chills
    • — Urinary changes (e.g., urinary frequency)
    • — Signs or symptoms of preterm labor
    • — Signs of hypertensive disorders
  • • Common Diagnostic Tests
    • — Maternal
      • • Urine screen
      • • Glucose tolerance test
      • • Uterine activity
    • — Fetal
      • • Quad screen includes alpha fetoprotein, human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), estriol, inhibin A; for high-risk pregnancy, chromosomal abnormalities, and neural tube defects
      • • Chorionic villi sampling (CVS) for genetic and chromosomal disorders at 8–12 weeks; full bladder required; Rh-negative mother requires RhoGAM (Rho[D] immune globulin) postprocedure
      • • Amniocentesis performed at 16 weeks to determine genetic disorders, at 30 weeks to determine lung maturity; bladder emptied if performed after 20 weeks’ gestation; Rh-negative mother requires RhoGAM postprocedure
      • • Ultrasound: Multiple purposes; client must have a full bladder
      • • Nonstress test (NST) via ultrasound transducer records fetal movement and heart rate after 28 weeks; increase in heart rate (reactivity) expected in healthy fetus
      • • Biophysical profile (via ultrasound): Includes NST results, amniotic fluid volume, fetal breathing movements, fetal tone and body movements
      • • Uterine activity (kick counts)

Labor

  • • True labor
    • — Pain in lower back radiating to the abdomen
    • — Regular, rhythmic contractions
    • — Increased intensity with ambulation
    • — Progressive cervical dilation and effacement
  • • False labor
    • — Discomfort localized to the abdomen
    • — No lower back pain
    • — Contractions often stop with ambulation or position change
  • • Stages of Labor
    • — First stage: Dilation and effacement with 100% cervical effacement and complete dilation of cervix (10 cm). Duration is from 8–20 hours in the primipara and 5–14 hours in the multipara. Includes two phases: latent (0 to 5 cm) and active (6 to 10 cm). Previously: three phases: Latent (0 to 3 cm), active (4 to 7 cm), and transition (8 to 10 cm)
    • — Second stage: Begins with complete cervical dilatation; expulsion ending with birth of the baby. Generally lasts from a few minutes to 2 hours
    • — Third stage: Placental separation. It begins with the birth of the baby and ends with the expulsion of the placenta. This process can last up to 30 minutes, with an average length of 5 to 10 minutes
    • — Fourth stage: Defined as the first 1–4 hours after delivery of the placenta. Monitor for excessive bleeding and uterine atony
  • • Labor Progression (Fig. 10.2)
    • — Cervical dilation: Stretching of the cervical os to allow passage of the infant (from 0–10 cm)
    • — Effacement: Thinning and shortening of the cervix (0%–100%)
    • — Station: Location of the presenting part in relation to the midpelvis or ischial spines, measured in centimeters above and below, using a scale from −5 to +5 or −3 to +3
      • • Station 0 = Engaged
      • • Station +2 = 2 cm below the level of the ischial spines
      • • +5 = Crowning
    • — Fetal presentation: Part of the fetus that presents to the inlet
    • — Position: Relationship of the point of reference (occiput sacrum, acromion) on the fetal presenting part to the mother’s pelvis
      • • Left occiput anterior (LOA): Most common position
    • — Lie: Relationship of the long axis (spine) of the fetus to the long axis (spine) of the mother
      • • Longitudinal: Up and down
      • • Transverse: Perpendicular
      • • Oblique: Slanted
    • — Attitude: Relationship of fetal parts to one another
      • • Flexion: Desired attitude, so that smallest diameter of the fetal head is presented
      • • Extension can cause dystocia
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Assessment
      • • Baseline maternal vital signs, including pain
      • • Medication history
      • • Physical assessment
      • • Food and fluid intake: Hydration and bladder status, need for catheterization, intravenous (IV) therapy
      • • Bowel elimination
      • • Ambulation and positioning: Upright, sitting, squatting (best position)
      • • Labs and diagnostic tests
      • • Emotional and cultural responses
    • — Labor Assessment
      • • Onset of labor and progression
      • • Vaginal exam (assess dilation, effacement, station, position, and fetal presentation; do not perform in the presence of bleeding, which could indicate placenta previa or abruption)
      • • Status of membranes
      • • Bloody “show”
      • • Fetal heart rate (FHR) pattern
      • • Complications: infection, pregnancy-induced hypertension (PIH) or gestational hypertension (GH), bleeding, prolapsed cord, fetal distress
    • — Assessment of contractions (uterine activity pattern)
      • • Duration: amount of time a contraction lasts, from the beginning to the end
      • • Frequency: The time from the beginning of one contraction to the beginning of the next contraction
      • • Intensity: Internal monitoring from 30 mm Hg (mild) to 70 mm Hg (strong)
      • • Resting tone/time: Tension of uterine muscle between contractions and time between contractions
    • — Fetal Heart Rate
      • • Characteristics of the FHR
      • • Normal range: 110–160 beats/min
      • • Tachycardia: >160 beats/min
      • • Bradycardia: <110 beats/min
      • • Variability: Change in the heart rate from beat to beat (short-term) and cyclic changes over time (long-term), in 3–5 cycles per minute; moderate long-term variability is reassuring
      • • Changes in FHR
      • • Accelerations: FHR increases with movement; reassuring
      • • — Decelerations:
      • • Early decelerations (reassuring) often occur during second stage and indicate head compression
      • • Late decelerations (nonreassuring) (even if not very “deep”) indicate placental insufficiency or hypoxia
      • • Variable decelerations (nonreassuring): May or may not be associated with contractions, indicate compression of umbilical cord
      • • The deceleration pattern indicates an insult to the fetus; the variability indicates how well the fetus is tolerating the insult
    • — Fetal Monitoring
      • • External fetal monitoring
      • • Is noninvasive and is performed with a Toco transducer or Doppler ultrasonic transducer
      • • Internal fetal monitoring
      • • Is invasive; the fetal membranes (amniotic membranes) must be ruptured and an electrode must be attached to the presenting part of the fetus

Postpartum Maternal Assessment Memory Tool: BUBBLE HA

  • •   
    • Breasts
      • • — Assess consistency (soft, firm, filling, engorged), nipples (intact, sore, flat, everted, or inverted), masses
    • Uterus: Fundal Involution
      • • — Immediately after delivery: The fundus is several centimeters below the umbilicus
      • • — Within 12 hours: The fundus rises to the umbilicus
      • • — Descends 1 cm (fingerbreadth) a day for 9–10 days; then the fundus is below the symphysis pubis
      • • — Should be midline and firm
    • Bladder
      • • — Measure output; assess for distention or retention
    • Bowel
      • • — Assess for distention, passing flatus, and bowel sounds
    • Lochia
      • • — Endometrial sloughing from rubra (red); serosa (pink); alba (white)
      • • — Assess color, odor, volume
    • Leg Assessment
      • • — Assess for signs of thrombosis
    • Episiotomy
      • • — Assess episiotomy or laceration repair for intactness, hematoma, edema, bruising, redness, and drainage
    • Hemorrhoids
      • • — Treat with: Sitz bath; Tucks; ointments
    • Attachment
      • • — Assess maternal-infant interaction for bonding behaviors

Teaching points for both parents (if present)

  • Primary focus is on signs of physical and emotional symptoms of potential problems for mother and infant
    • • —  Change peri pads as needed and with voiding/defecation
    • • — Wipe perineum front to back
    • • — Good handwashing technique
    • • — Ice packs, sitz baths, peri bottle lavage, and topical anesthetic spray and pads for perineal swelling or episiotomy care
    • • — Breastfeeding instructions
    • • —  Healthy diet and adequate fluid intake
    • • — Rest/nap when the baby sleeps
    • • — Contraceptive use
  • Reinforce teaching points
    • • — RhoGAM
    • • Administered to Rh-negative women with possible exposure to Rh-positive blood such as: after CVS, amniocentesis, miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, abortion, any uterine bleed during pregnancy, and childbirth
    • • Should have negative indirect Coombs test
    • • Administered IM at 26–28 weeks gestation and within 72 hours after delivery (only if baby’s blood type is found to be positive)
    • • Compatibility assessment performed by two nurses (blood product)
    • • — Rubella Vaccine
    • • Given subcutaneously to the nonimmune client before discharge from hospital
    • • May breastfeed
    • • Do not administer if the client or family member is immunocompromised
    • • Avoid pregnancy for 2–3 months (reinforce contraception)

Complications of Childbearing

  • • Chronic Hypertension
    • — Hypertension in pregnant woman:
      • • Chronic hypertension before 20 weeks of gestation
      • • Persistent after 12 weeks postpartum
  • • Superimposed Preeclampsia or Eclampsia
    • — Development of preeclampsia or eclampsia in a woman with chronic hypertension before 20 weeks of gestation
  • • Gestational Hypertension (GH)
    • — Hypertension in pregnant women without proteinuria after 20 weeks
    • — Risk factors: obesity, prior kidney disorders, AMA (advanced maternal age), multiple pregnancies such as twins or triplets

Preeclampsia

  • •   
    • — Hypertension with proteinuria
    • — Risk factors: nulliparity, AMA, placental problems, history of prior hypertension, family history of preeclampsia
    • — Preeclampsia symptoms
      • • Blood pressure (BP)
      • • Mild: Rise to 30 mm Hg systolic or 15 mm Hg diastolic over baseline, or 140/90 or greater
      • • Severe: BP of 160/110 mm Hg, when either systolic reaches 160 mm Hg or diastolic reaches 110 mm Hg, or both occur simultaneously; can be confirmed quickly
      • • Proteinuria
      • • Mild: ≥1+ (≥0.3 g in a 24-hour specimen)
      • • Severe: >2+ (2 g in a 24-hour specimen)
      • • Edema
      • • Mild: Eyes, face, fingers
      • • Severe: Generalized edema
      • • Deep tendon reflex (DTR)
      • • Mild: May be normal
      • • Severe: 3+ or greater and clonus
      • • Central nervous system (CNS) symptoms
      • • Mild: Headache, irritability
      • • Severe: Severe headache, visual disturbances
      • • Other
      • • Weight gain >2 lb (0.9 kg)/week; oliguria <100 mL/4 hours; epigastric pain, ↑ creatinine, thrombocytopenia, increased serum glutamic-oxaloacetic transaminase (SGOT)
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management: Pre-eclampsia
      • • ↓ Stimulation in room
      • • Explain procedures
      • • Maintain IV (16- to 18-g venocatheter)
      • • Monitor BP every 15 to 30 minutes
      • • Monitor urine for protein every 1 hour
      • • The health care provider (HCP) may prescribe magnesium sulfate
  • • — Preeclampsia progresses to eclampsia when seizures are present  
    • — Nursing and Collaborative Management: Eclampsia (Seizures)
      • • Remember that seizures can occur postpartum
      • • Notify HCP when the client starts having seizures
      • • Stay with client
      • • Reposition the client to side
      • • Administer oxygen
      • • Have suction available at the bedside
      • • Prepare to administer magnesium sulfate bolus

Gestational Diabetes

  • • Screening
    • — 1-hour glucose screen between 24 and 26 weeks
  • • Diagnosis: Blood glucose level is higher than 140 mg/dL (7.8 mmol/L) 1 hour after consuming glucose solution.
    • — Goal: Strict blood glucose control
    • — Generally, glyburide or insulin is used during pregnancy
      • • Glyburide is classified as a Category C by the U.S. FDA for use in pregnancy
    • — Insulin does not cross the placenta, and glyburide only minimally crosses it

Preterm Labor (PTL)

  • • Signs and Symptoms
    • — More than five contractions in an hour
    • — Menstrual-like cramps
    • — Low, dull backache
    • — Pelvic pressure
    • — Increase/change in vaginal discharge
    • — Leaking or gush of amniotic fluid
  • • Tocolytics: Administration
    • — Terbutaline
      • • Side effects
      • • Nervousness
      • • Tremulousness
      • • Headache
      • • Nausea and vomiting (N/V), diarrhea
      • • Epigastric pain
      • • Adverse effects
      • • Maternal pulse >130 beats/min; arrhythmias
      • • BP <90/60 mm Hg
      • • Chest pain
      • • Pulmonary edema
      • • Hypokalemia
      • • Hyperglycemia
      • • FHR >180 beats/min
      • • Nursing interventions
      • • Assess hyperglycemia in clients treated with steroids
      • • I&O, daily weight
      • • Antidote
      • • Propranolol
    • — Magnesium sulfate
      • • Side effects
      • • CNS depression
      • • ↓ Respirations
      • • ↓ Deep tendon reflexes (DTRs)
      • • Adverse effects
      • • ↓ Urine output
      • • Pulmonary edema
      • • Nursing interventions
      • • Hold if R <12, urine output <100 mL/4 hours, absent DTRs
      • • Monitor magnesium levels
      • • Antidote
      • • Calcium gluconate
  • • Other Drugs Used to Decrease Contractions
    • — Indomethacin, naproxen
    • — Fenoprofen
    • — Nifedipine
    • — Nicardipine
  • • Drug to Enhance Fetal Lung Maturity
    • — Betamethasone
      • • Administered if fetus is <35 weeks’ gestation

Spontaneous Abortion (Fig. 10.4)

  • • Assessment
    • — Vaginal bleeding at week 20 or less of gestation
    • — Uterine cramping, backache, and pelvic pressure
    • — May have signs and symptoms of shock
    • — Assess client’s and family members’ emotional status, needs, and provide support
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Monitor VS, LOC, and amount of bleeding
    • — Prepare the client to receive IV fluids or PRBCs
    • — If the client is Rh negative, administer RhoGAM
    • — Assess grieving process

Incompetent Cervix

  • • Recurrent premature dilation of the cervix; is defined as passive and painless dilation of the cervix during the second trimester
  • • Conservative Management
    • — Bed rest
    • — Hydration
    • — Tocolysis (inhibition of uterine contractions)
  • • Cervical Cerclage May Be Performed
    • — Postoperative nursing care: monitor uterine activity, bleeding, and rupture of membranes, and signs of infection
    • — Administer tocolytics
    • — Discharge teaching: teach to report signs of labor, bleeding, and infection; decrease activity; pelvic rest
    • — If vaginal delivery is planned, cervical cerclage is left in place until 37 weeks; may be left in if cesarian birth is planned

Ectopic Pregnancy (Fig. 10.5)

  • • Assessment
    • — Missed period, but early signs of pregnancy absent
    • — Positive pregnancy test
  • • Rupture
    • — Sharp, unilateral pelvic pain
    • — Vaginal bleeding
    • — Referred shoulder pain
    • — Syncope
    • — Shock
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Monitor hemodynamic status
    • — Prepare client for surgery and/or administration of IV fluids and PRBCs

Abruptio Placentae and Placenta Previa (Fig. 10.6)

  • • Abruptio Placentae
    • — Bleeding: Concealed or overt
    • — Uterine tone: Tense without relaxation, to rigid and board-like
    • — Pain: Persistently painful
    • — FHR: Abnormal (complete abruption = absent FHR)
  • • Placenta Previa
    • — Bleeding: Bright red vaginal (usually in third trimester)
    • — Uterine tone: Soft
    • — Pain: Painless
    • — FHR: Usually normal unless bleeding is severe and mother becomes hypovolemic

    • —Risk factors for DIC in pregnancy:
      • • Fetal demise
      • • Infection/sepsis

Dystocia

  • • A difficult birth resulting from problems involving the “5 Ps” (powers, passage, passenger, psyche, and position). Examples include a lack of progress in cervical dilation, delay in fetal descent, cephalopelvic disproportion, or change in the characteristics of uterine contraction.

Postpartum Complications

  • • Hemorrhage
    • — Assess fundal location and consistency
    • — Assess vaginal bleeding; saturating one pad/hour indicates hemorrhage
    • — Monitor for signs and symptoms of shock
    • — Assess for bladder distention; can prevent involution and lead to hemorrhage
  • • Venous Thromboembolism
    • — Related to venous stasis and hypercoagulability
    • — Can result in superficial venous thrombosis, deep vein thrombosis, or pulmonary embolism
    • — Assess legs for pain, tenderness, swelling (Homans sign not reliable)
    • — Treatment: Bed rest, anticoagulants, leg elevation, and analgesia
  • • Infection
    • — Perineal infections
    • — Endometritis
    • — Parametritis
    • — Peritonitis
    • — Mastitis
    • — Cystitis
    • — Pyelonephritis
    • — HIV, hepatitis, other sexually transmitted infections

Newborn Parameters (Approximate for Full Term) (Fig. 10.7)

  • • Length: 18–22 inches (45.72–55.88 cm)
  • • Weight: 5.5–9.5 lb (2.49–4.31 kg)
  • • Head circumference: 13.2–14 inches (33.53–35.56 cm)
  • • Sutures palpable with fontanels
  • • Fontanel closure
    • — Anterior: By 18 months
    • — Posterior: 6–8 weeks
  • • Umbilical cord should have three vessels—two arteries and one vein
    • — A two-vessel cord may be indicative of an abnormality or associated with chromosomal and renal defects
  • • Extremities should be flexed
  • • Developmental dysplasia of hip
  • • — Major gluteal folds should be even
  • • — Check for Ortolani sign and Barlow sign
  • • Creases should be present on soles of feet
  • • Pulses should be palpable (radial, brachial, femoral)
  • • Nursing and Collaborative Management
    • — Keep the newborn warm
    • — Suction airway as needed
    • — Assess for respiratory distress
    • — Normal or physiological jaundice appears after the first 24 hours in full-term newborns
    • — Pathological jaundice occurs before this time and may indicate early hemolysis of red blood cells
    • — Assess the hemoglobin and hematocrit (H & H) and blood glucose levels
    • — Weigh daily
    • — Monitor intake and output; weigh diapers if necessary (1 g = 1 mL of urine)
    • — Monitor temperature
    • — Observe for any cracks in skin
    • — Administer eye medication within 1 hour after birth
    • — Provide cord care
    • — Provide circumcision care; teach client how to care for circumcision site
    • — Supine position (“back to sleep”) to prevent sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS)
    • — Observe for normal stool and passage of meconium
    • — Assess the newborn’s reflexes
  • • Newborn Reflexes
    • — Ages at which infant reflexes disappear
      • • 3–4 months
      • • Sucking or rooting
      • • Moro
      • • Tonic neck or fencing
      • • Stepping or walking
      • • 8 months
      • • Palmar-plantar grasp
      • • 1 year–18 months
      • • Babinski sign

    3. 3. Assess the heart rate and perform chest compressions if rate is below 60 beats/min.
    4. 4. Dry the infant and place him or her under a radiant warmer or skin-to-skin with the mother.

Major Newborn Complications

  • • Hypoglycemia
    • — Assess for risk factors in maternal history (infant of diabetic mother) and environmental factors (cold stress)
    • — In general, blood glucose levels <40 mg/dL (2.22 mmol/L) are considered abnormal and warrant investigation and intervention
    • — Infant is fed formula or breastfed as soon as the repeat (usually) sample is obtained to prevent a further drop in blood glucose
    • — Administer IV glucose infusions per HCP prescription for infants with symptomatic hypoglycemia or those too ill to be safely fed orally
    • — Reduce adverse environmental factors (e.g., irritability and hypothermia)
  • • Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)
    • — Caused by inability to produce surfactant
    • — Resulting in hypoxia and acidosis
  • • Meconium Aspiration Syndrome
    • — Fetal distress increases intestinal peristalsis
    • — Releases meconium into the amniotic fluid
    • — Monitor respiratory rate, breathing effort, color
  • • Retinopathy of Prematurity
    • — Damage to retinal vessels caused by prolonged use of oxygen (>30 days)
    • — Infants receiving oxygen should be examined by an ophthalmologist
  • • Hyperbilirubinemia
    • — ↑ Serum levels >12 mg/dL (205 µmol)
    • — Crucial to prevention of kernicterus, which results in permanent neurological damage
    • — Jaundice starts at the head, spreads to the chest, abdomen, arms, legs, hands, and feet
    • — Phototherapy: Use of fluorescent lights or phototherapy blanket to reduce serum bilirubin levels
      • • Possible adverse effects: Eye damage, dehydration, or sensory deprivation
      • • Expose the skin but cover the genital area
      • • Cover eyes with eye shields
      • • Monitor skin temperature, keep room warm
      • • Increase fluids to compensate for H2O loss, and to facilitate passage of bilirubin in stool
      • • Expect loose green stools and urine
      • • Monitor skin color
      • • Reposition every 2 hours and as needed (PRN)
      • • Provide stimulation
      • • Continue monitoring for signs of rebound hyperbilirubinemia
  • • Erythroblastosis Fetalis
    • — Results from a Rh-negative mother exposed to Rh-positive blood and develops antibodies that are passed to the fetus, destroying fetal red blood cells
    • — Characterized by hemolytic anemia or hyperbilirubinemia
    • — Exchange of fetal and maternal blood occurs at birth
    • — Administer Rho(D) immune globulin (RhoGAM) to a Rh-negative mother to prevent the development of maternal antibodies
    • — Treatment of newborn includes phototherapy and exchange transfusions
  • • Sepsis
    • — Presence of bacteria in the blood
    • — Prevention: Antibiotics in labor for group B Streptococcus (GBS)–positive test
    • — Assess for maternal, intrapartum, and neonatal risk factors
    • — Early signs are nonspecific and include lethargy, poor feeding, poor weight gain, and irritability
  • • TORCH Infections
    • — Infections that are caused by one of the following:
      • • Toxoplasmosis
      • • Other infections (e.g., gonorrhea, syphilis, varicella, hepatitis B, HIV, or human parvovirus B19)
      • • Rubella
      • • Cytomegalovirus
      • • Herpes simplex virus
  • • Maternal Substance Abuse
    • — Effects on the fetus vary according to substance
    • — Narcotics cause passive addiction: Neonatal abstinence syndrome
    • — Cocaine is classified as a narcotic but not an opioid; it causes vasoconstriction, preterm birth, poor growth, and neurological problems
    • — Treatment of drug exposed newborns focuses on reduction of external stimuli, supportive treatment of symptoms, and sedation
  • • Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASDs)
    • — Caused by maternal alcohol use during pregnancy
    • — Associated with neurological problems, IQ deficit, and attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder
    • — Craniofacial features include microcephaly, small eyes or short palpebral fissures, a thin upper lip, a flat midface, and an indistinct philtrum (Fig. 10.8)
  • • Maternal HIV
    • — Monitor antibodies closely throughout pregnancy
    • — Testing and treatment in pregnancy greatly reduce risk of transmission to fetus
  • • Maternal Diabetes
    • — Infant born to mother with type 1 or type 2 diabetes or gestational diabetes
    • — Newborn may have hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, polycythemia, hyperbilirubinemia, cardiomyopathy, respiratory distress syndrome (RDS), birth trauma, and congenital anomalies
    • — Macrosomia: Large for gestational age (LGA) macrosomic infants often have respiratory disorders and other problems

Test-Taking Strategy

Bowtie questions are unique and can test multiple cognitive skills. First, always read the question carefully and make sure that you have a good understanding of what is being asked. Second, read the scenario and all related client information provided in all of the chart tabs. The clue to help you decide which client condition is being experienced may be embedded within the client data that is presented in the chart. Be thorough and don’t assume you know what is going on until you carefully read the information presented. Third, review the options provided. Hopefully, the disease process you were thinking of is listed. If so, trust your gut. If you were not thinking about one of the options provided, look at each option individually and review the client information. This should assist you in deciding on the correct option. Once you have decided on a client condition, analyze the information you know about the diagnosis to decide which action to take and which parameters to monitor.

Next Generation NCLEX® Examination (NGN)–Style Question

Bowtie

Scenario: The RN in the labor and delivery unit is caring for a client who is being brought to triage. The client is arriving via ambulance following a motor vehicle accident (MVA) with airbag deployment. She is reportedly 33 weeks’ gestation and is a G1P0. The client reports possibly losing consciousness immediately after the accident. Stated when she awoke, there was a small pool of blood under her. The pants the client is wearing have obvious signs of bleeding. The client appears very anxious and pale. She is not speaking much and only answers yes or no questions. She reports mild continuous “tightness” in abdomen. She denies contractions. She also reports that she may have felt the fetus move earlier that morning but is not sure. She denies any complications with the pregnancy up to this point.

Complete the diagram by choosing from the options below to identify which condition the client is most likely experiencing. Then choose two actions the RN will take to address the identified condition and two parameters that the RN will monitor to assess the client’s progress.

Answer:

Rationale: The client in the scenario is most likely experiencing an abruptio placentae. Actions the RN should take include applying fetal monitors to evaluate fetal heart tones and preparing the client for a probable cesarean delivery. The RN would also evaluate fetal heart tones observing for any signs of fetal distress such as late decelerations. These observations will help determine the degree of abruption, assess the condition of both the mother and the fetus, and determine how quickly the infant should be delivered.
